I've been informed by my spouse that N+1 bikes is not currently authorized, but I want to have some off-road capability. I also don't want to lose the ability to go quite fast on the road. Interchangeable wheelsets and minimal other changes is the way to go!
<!--more-->

***This post is a bit of a work in progress.***

### Parts:
- DT Swiss Spline C1800 Wheels https://www.dtswiss.com/en/products/wheels-road/cross-road/c-1800-spline/c-1800-spline-23/
- Panaracer GravelKing SK 35mm tires https://www.panaracer.com/lineup/gravel.html
- Tubeless Sealant
- Absolute Black Sub-Compact 46/30 https://absoluteblack.cc/oval-road-chainrings-30-46-and-32-48-for-110-4bcd/
- Sunrace CSRX8 11-42T Road Cassette http://www.sunrace.com/en/products/detail/csrx8
- Shimano 160mm brake rotors


### Why These Parts

Putting everything into a second wheelset lets me swap between the gravel and road configurations pretty easily. I picked the Spline C1800 wheels because they are essentially the offroad (Cross) version of the Spline 1800 wheels that came with the bike and which have been good for the past thousand miles or so I've put on in the year I've owned the Canyon.

The elephant here is going to be the sub-compact chainrings. I can't take them off quite as easily when switching, and many people would say a compact is good enough.

Here's the simple truth, while I'm slowly losing weight and slowly adding power, I'm a heavy rider with a low power output. My health means I can't make significant gains in either of those numbers over the medium term.

I started with http://bikecalculator.com/ which allows you to tweak a variety of variables and see how it affects everything else. I worked out a rough starting point, I can put out 150w for about 20 minutes, I can also balance at about 3mph. This means I can for a mile on a grade of about 8%.
<a href="/assets/images/posts/gravel-bike/gearing_calculator_46_30-11_42.png" target="_blank"><img src="/assets/images/posts/gravel-bike/gearing_calculator_46_30-11_42.png" alt="speed calculator - 3.13mph at 150w on 8% grade" title="3.13mph at 150w on 8% grade" style="max-width: 100%; max-height: 500px"></a>

A 42T sprocket that I can comfortably fit on my bike with a derailure hanger extender only takes me down to 3.28mph, a bit faster than my goal. That's also at 50rpm which is spinning more slowly than I'm really comfortable with.
<a href="/assets/images/posts/gravel-bike/gearing_calculator_46_30-11_42.png" target="_blank"><img src="/assets/images/posts/gravel-bike/gearing_calculator_46_30-11_42.png" alt="gearing calculator - 34x42 = 3.28mph @ 50rpm" title="34x42 = 3.28mph @ 50rpm" style="max-width: 100%; max-height: 500px"></a>

Dropping down to 30T at the front gets me below the 3mph goal. It's still at 50rpm, but with the actual numbers will be closer to 55rpm. I think I can keep that up for a while.
<a href="/assets/images/posts/gravel-bike/gearing_calculator_46_30-11_42.png" target="_blank"><img src="/assets/images/posts/gravel-bike/gearing_calculator_46_30-11_42.png" alt="gearing calculator - 30x42 = 2.88mph @ 50rpm" title="30x42 = 2.88mph @ 50rpm" style="max-width: 100%; max-height: 500px"></a>

What do I lose with the change? Approximately 3mph of top end speed. Lets also be honest with myself here, I've only pushed the 50x11 a handful of times in the past year. My weight alone means I quickly get faster than a comfortable pedalling cadence on even a fairly minor descent and am leaning on the brake rotors anyway. I'll definitely be sticking with the 160mm rotors for heat disipation.
