One of the most useful things I ever did for my technology career was leave technology for a while.

I built and ran a specialty retail business, Sheep's Clothing, from the ground up. We sold yarn, fiber, and knitting supplies to a customer base that knew exactly what it liked and was perfectly capable of telling us when we got something wrong.

Retail teaches you something that is oddly hard to learn inside most technology organizations: what it actually feels like to be on the customer side of the counter.

<!--more-->

In software and infrastructure, customer feedback usually arrives after being filtered through support queues, tickets, dashboards, product managers, and internal narratives. By the time it reaches the people making decisions, it has often been translated several times and partially domesticated.

Retail does not allow that distance.

When a customer cannot find what they need, the confusion is visible. When a store layout is wrong, you watch people hesitate in the wrong place. When someone has a bad experience, they do not file a Jira ticket. They look at you and tell you.

That level of immediacy changes your standards permanently.

## Distance Makes Rationalization Easy

The dangerous thing about distance from customers is not just that it slows feedback loops. It makes self-protection easier.

In technology, it is very easy to explain away customer friction. Maybe the user misunderstood. Maybe support framed it badly. Maybe this is an edge case. Maybe the complaint says more about the user than the product.

Some of that is occasionally true. Most of the time, it is defensive storytelling.

In retail, defensive storytelling survives only until the next person walks in and has the same problem.

That was one of the most valuable corrections I got from running a store. It made me much less tolerant of interpretive distance between builders and users. If the people making decisions are too far from the customer experience, they will eventually start designing for their own internal logic instead of the customer's actual reality.

## Behavior Matters More Than Declarations

Retail also taught me to trust observed behavior more than stated preference.

Customers are usually sincere when they tell you what they want. They are just not always accurate. They describe solutions when what they actually feel is a problem. They ask for features when what they need is a better outcome. They say one thing and then consistently buy, avoid, linger over, or ignore something else.

We saw this constantly in the store. Customers would express enthusiasm about product categories that did not move when we expanded them. They would also gravitate toward arrangements or experiences they had never explicitly requested. If we had listened only to stated preference, we would have made worse decisions.

That lesson travels directly into product and technology leadership. Feature requests matter. So do complaints. But neither should be taken literally without looking at the behavior underneath them.

The useful question is not "what did they ask for?" It is "what problem are they revealing?"

## Complaints Are a Privilege

Retail also cured me of the instinct to resent the complaining customer.

The customer who takes the time to complain is still in relationship with you. They are giving you a chance to see what is broken. The ones you should worry about are the ones who had the same experience and quietly decided not to return.

Technology teams often get this exactly backwards. They treat criticism as noise, defend the product, or explain why the user experience was reasonable from the inside. That reflex is expensive.

Complaints are not always correct in their proposed solutions, but they are often correct that something about the experience failed.

Once you have spent enough time face-to-face with customers, it becomes harder to hide behind internal explanations. You start to value the unpleasant signal because it is still signal.

## Customer Empathy Is Not Soft

I dislike the way customer empathy is sometimes described as though it were a niceness layer added on top of "serious" execution.

It is not soft. It is diagnostic.

Direct contact with customers gives you information that organizations otherwise spend a lot of money trying to reconstruct indirectly. It shows you where your language is unclear, where your assumptions are wrong, where your process creates friction, and where your internal categories do not match the way customers actually experience the world.

That kind of information changes product decisions. It changes interface decisions. It changes support design. It changes what you prioritize and what you stop building.

It also changes how you lead. Once you have spent enough time in close contact with customers, it becomes harder to celebrate technical elegance that does not cash out as usefulness.

## The Technology Version of Standing Behind the Counter

Most technology leaders do not have the structural advantage of running a store. They have to build customer proximity deliberately.

That means going farther than NPS dashboards and quarterly summaries. Sit in on support calls. Watch people use the product in their real environment. Talk to the teams hearing objections every day. Ask what customers repeatedly misunderstand. Ask what they try to do that your product quietly makes harder than it should.

The phrase Steve Blank popularized, "get out of the building," remains useful because it names a discipline that organizations drift away from very easily.

Retail is what "out of the building" looks like when you do not have a choice.

## What It Changed for Me

Running a retail business made me more skeptical of organizational distance, more attentive to observed behavior, and more suspicious of explanations that sounded tidy from the inside but did not match what customers were actually experiencing.

It also made me much less romantic about customer-centricity as a slogan. Most organizations say they care about customers. Fewer are willing to put decision-makers close enough to customer reality to be corrected by it.

That correction is uncomfortable. It is also where better judgment comes from.

I learned a great deal in retail that had nothing to do with yarn and a great deal to do with technology. The biggest lesson was simple: if you want to build better systems, spend more time where the consequences of your decisions are impossible to ignore.
