Remote work did not create most of the leadership problems people associate with it.

It exposed them.

Plenty of teams looked healthy in an office because the office supplied a constant stream of ambient reassurance. People were visibly busy. Questions got answered in hallways. Managers could mistake proximity for alignment. Culture spread through osmosis, or at least enough of it did that weak systems were harder to notice.

Once teams became distributed, a lot of that disappeared. What remained was the actual quality of leadership, communication, trust, and operating discipline.

That is why some remote teams worked surprisingly well and others felt as if they were coming apart. The difference was rarely the video platform. It was whether the team already knew how to function with clarity.

## What the Office Used to Hide

I have managed teams across different structures, including teams where distance was minor, teams where it was material, and teams where hybrid work created two different experiences of the same company.

The recurring pattern is this: co-location lets organizations get away with a lot.

It lets managers be vague, because people can chase clarification informally. It lets decisions stay half-documented, because enough of the right people were in the room when the decision happened. It lets cultural drift go unnoticed, because people still feel connected enough to tolerate it for a while.

Remote work removes those cushions.

If priorities are fuzzy, it becomes obvious faster. If writing is poor, people stall. If trust is weak, surveillance starts to look attractive. If recognition depends on who gets seen in casual conversation, remote people fall behind quickly.

That is why I do not think of remote work as a fundamentally different management discipline. I think of it as a harsher test of the one you already have.

[Prithwiraj Choudhury's work on work-from-anywhere](https://www.library.hbs.edu/working-knowledge/in-a-work-from-anywhere-world-how-remote-will-workers-go) is useful here because it frames remote flexibility as an operating-model question, not just a perk. The productivity gains he and his coauthors found at the USPTO did not come from vibes. They came from a system designed to support the work.

## The Real Foundation Is Writing

The best distributed teams I have seen were almost always better-than-average writing cultures.

That does not mean every engineer was a beautiful prose stylist. It means the team could reliably do a few important things in writing:

- state what was decided
- explain why it was decided
- make ownership visible
- surface blockers early
- hand work from one person or time zone to another without loss of context

Teams that cannot do those things in writing end up compensating with meetings, and too many meetings are often a sign that the system underneath is weak.

A distributed team should not need a call to answer every ordinary question. If it does, either the decision rights are unclear, the documentation is weak, or the team has not learned how to communicate with enough precision to work asynchronously.

That is not a remote-work issue. That is an operational discipline issue.

## Trust Has to Move from Presence to Outcomes

Bad remote management often starts from a faulty question: how do I know people are really working?

That question usually belongs to a manager who was relying, consciously or not, on physical visibility as a proxy for control.

The better question is: do I have a team design that makes good work legible?

Can I see what matters? Are goals clear? Are commitments explicit? Do people surface trouble early? Are decisions and handoffs visible enough that I can tell whether the team is functioning without needing to watch them type?

Once you build around those signals, the need for surveillance mostly disappears. And if you feel you still need surveillance, that is often evidence that something more basic is broken.

Trust in distributed teams is not soft. It is built through clear expectations, reliable follow-through, and leaders who do what they said they would do.

## Hybrid Is Harder Than Either Side Admits

Fully remote and fully in-person teams each have their own logic. Hybrid teams often inherit the weaknesses of both.

The problem is not simply location. It is unequal access.

When some people are in the room and others are on screens, the room usually wins unless the system is explicitly designed otherwise. Informal conversations stay local. Small decisions happen before or after the meeting. Remote contributors get heard, but not always incorporated. Recognition follows visibility, and visibility follows proximity.

Over time, this creates a two-tier organization whether leadership intended it or not.

The most effective mitigation I know is to design from the remote case outward. Document decisions as though half the relevant people were not in the room, because in practice they often are not. Make written updates normal. Make ownership explicit. Avoid letting important context live only in physical adjacency.

If you design for the in-office case and expect remote people to adapt, you are not running a hybrid organization. You are running an in-office organization with a tolerated remote exception.

## Culture Becomes More Deliberate

One thing offices do well is transmit norms without having to name them. People watch what senior leaders do. They see who gets listened to. They notice what behavior gets rewarded.

Distributed teams lose much of that passive transmission, which means culture has to become more explicit.

That includes:

- how disagreement is handled
- how bad news is surfaced
- how recognition happens
- what responsiveness is expected
- what kind of boundaries are respected

Leaders who do not make those norms legible tend to assume the team "just knows." In distributed settings, that assumption breaks down quickly.

This is one reason I think manager one-on-ones matter even more in remote and hybrid systems. In an office, you can detect strain or confusion from the edges. At a distance, you often have to ask directly.

## What Actually Works

The teams I trust most in distributed settings usually share a few traits.

They write things down before they have to. They use meetings for work that genuinely benefits from live interaction. They keep goals and ownership visible. They do not confuse availability with contribution. They take handoffs seriously. And their leaders do not bypass the system whenever urgency rises.

That last point matters. If the formal process applies only when things are calm, it is not the process. It is decoration.

Remote and hybrid teams do not need more performative connection or more software. They need more intentional operating habits.

## The Useful Reframe

I do not think remote work is magic, and I do not think it is ruinous.

I think it is clarifying.

It widens the aperture on geography, hiring, and flexibility. It also makes weak leadership easier to see. For some organizations that is uncomfortable, but it is still useful. It tells you where your real dependencies were.

The leaders who do well in remote and hybrid environments are usually the ones who accept that some of the old shortcuts are gone and that this is mostly a gain, not a loss. If the team only worked because everyone was in the same building, the team did not really work as well as it looked.

That is the lesson I keep coming back to. Remote work did not repeal the fundamentals. It revealed them.
