Years ago, I spent a long time standing in the cold waiting for a shot that might not happen.

The light was wrong when I arrived. The composition was cluttered. People kept walking into the frame. I could have taken a technically competent photo in the first five minutes and gone home with something perfectly acceptable. Instead I stayed, watched the scene change, moved three feet, changed lenses twice, and waited for the version that actually said what I wanted it to say.

That habit turned out to matter in leadership more than I expected.

Photography has never been separate from the rest of my life. I have taken it seriously for decades, won awards for it, and spent enough time behind a camera to know that most bad photographs are not technical failures. They are failures of seeing. The subject is there. The equipment is fine. What is missing is judgment.

Leadership problems often work the same way.

## Focal Length Is a Leadership Skill

One of the most useful things photography teaches is that perspective is a choice.

With a wide lens, you see context. Relationships. Background. The way one object changes the meaning of another. With a longer lens, you isolate. You remove noise. You pay attention to the one expression, the one gesture, the one detail that tells you what is really happening.

Leadership requires the same switch constantly.

At enterprise scale, I had days when I needed to stay wide. What is the business trying to do this quarter? Which teams are overloaded? Where are we building one-off solutions that will become operating problems six months from now? If you stay too tight at that moment, you solve a local issue and miss the system that created it.

I also had days when I needed to go long-lens immediately. Which engineer is silently carrying too much? Which project is slipping because no one wants to say the requirements are incoherent? Which "minor exception" is about to become a permanent support burden? If you stay wide for too long, you talk strategy while the actual problem walks out the door.

The mistake is not choosing one perspective over the other. The mistake is getting trapped in one.

I have seen technically strong leaders live permanently in telephoto. They know every tactical detail and miss the directional shift that makes those details irrelevant. I have also seen leaders live permanently in wide angle. They speak fluently about vision while the team underneath them is drowning in obvious friction.

The discipline is to ask, in the moment: am I too close to see the system, or too far back to see the problem?

## Most Bad Decisions Are Taken Too Early

Photography also taught me patience, and not in a mystical sense.

Beginners shoot too soon. They see something promising, worry the moment will disappear, and press the shutter before they understand the scene. Experienced photographers still move quickly when they need to, but they are usually slower to commit. They watch. They wait for a face to turn, for a shadow to clear, for the background to simplify, for the image to become legible.

Leadership pressure pushes in the opposite direction. A meeting is tense. A stakeholder wants an answer. A team wants clarity. There is enormous emotional reward in ending ambiguity quickly, even if the decision is premature.

I have made decisions too early for exactly that reason. Not because I lacked intelligence or data, but because waiting was uncomfortable.

What I learned over time is that some decisions improve dramatically if you let them develop for a little while longer. Not forever. Not into paralysis. Just long enough to find out whether the urgency is real or merely social.

Photography gave me a useful internal question: am I acting because the moment is right, or because I am tired of waiting?

That question has saved me from more than one bad call.

## Observation Beats Assumption

The camera punishes projection. You think you know what is in front of you, then you look at the frame and realize you were looking at the idea of the scene instead of the scene itself.

Leadership has the same trap.

Organizations are full of stories people tell themselves. The project is on track. The team is aligned. The customer understands the constraint. Everyone is bought in. Then you look a little harder and find the shadow in the corner of the frame: the one team that was never consulted, the one manager translating everything into softer language, the one exception process that is quietly undoing the standard.

Some of the most important leadership work I have done came from noticing things that did not fit the stated narrative.

An engineer who was too calm in a postmortem and therefore probably exhausted. A design review where the most junior person in the room was visibly right and knew not to push. A customer request that sounded urgent until you watched how the work would actually land on the teams expected to support it.

Photography trained me to distrust the first read. Look again. Change angle. Check the edges of the frame. See what the first glance edited out.

That is not an artistic flourish. It is operationally useful.

## Composition Is Mostly Exclusion

People new to photography often think composition means adding interest. In practice it often means removing distraction.

Move left. Lower the camera. Wait for the background to clear. Do not include the extra tree, the bright sign, the second subject, the horizon line that cuts through the head. The image gets stronger when you stop insisting that everything important must fit.

Leadership strategy works the same way.

A surprising number of organizational problems are really composition problems. Too many projects. Too many priorities. Too many exceptions. Too many communication paths. Too many people allowed to interrupt the same high-context work. Everyone feels busy. Very little is actually clear.

Some of the best decisions I have made as a leader felt, in the moment, like subtracting rather than building. Narrowing intake. Protecting focus. Standardizing where someone wanted customization. Telling a customer, a partner, or an executive that we were not going to put one more object into an already overloaded frame.

That kind of exclusion can feel unhelpful when judged locally. It is often the only thing that makes the broader picture legible.

## Why This Still Matters to Me

I do not think photography made me wise. I do think it gave me durable habits of mind.

Change focal length deliberately. Wait longer than your discomfort prefers. Observe before explaining. Cut what does not belong.

Those habits have helped me in strategy, in organizational design, in staffing, in incident analysis, and in the thousand smaller judgment calls that make up leadership in practice.

That is one reason I distrust the idea that our non-technical pursuits are separate from our technical or managerial lives. If you take a craft seriously enough, it changes how you see. And if it changes how you see, it eventually changes how you lead.
