Early in my career, the senior technology leader was usually the person with the deepest technical answers. If the network was unstable, the architecture was unclear, or the vendor was overselling, that person was expected to know more than everyone else in the room and settle the question.

That model made sense when technology was still treated as a specialized function. If IT was largely there to keep systems running, then the senior technology role naturally belonged to the strongest technologist.

That is not the job I ended up doing.

The shift did not happen all at once. It happened meeting by meeting. A budget discussion where the important question was not whether the design was elegant, but whether the spend was justified. A staffing problem where the technical answer was obvious but the organizational answer was not. A board or executive conversation where what mattered was not explaining the system in detail, but making the tradeoff legible to people who had to make a business decision.

That is when the role changed for me. The value was no longer primarily in having the deepest answer. It was in making good choices under constraint, building the right structure around the work, and translating technology into business consequences without flattening the truth.

## The Old Version of the Job

The older model of technology leadership was built around authority through expertise.

You owned infrastructure. You made architecture calls. You were the escalation point. You negotiated with vendors, approved designs, and decided what was technically sound enough to proceed. If you were good, the organization trusted your judgment because you understood the systems more deeply than anyone else available to them.

There is still a version of that role today, and in some environments it is exactly what is needed. If the organization is small enough, or the technology surface narrow enough, the senior leader may still need to be the most technically capable person in the room.

But once technology becomes central to growth, customer experience, risk, and capital allocation, that version of the job becomes insufficient.

## What Actually Changed

The biggest change was not that technology became more important. It was that technology stopped being separable from the rest of the business.

When I was younger, it was still possible in many companies to think of technology as a support function. Important, yes. Expensive, certainly. But still something adjacent to strategy rather than inside it.

That stopped being true. Customer experience became software-mediated. Operational efficiency became systems-dependent. Security and compliance became board-level concerns. Infrastructure decisions started carrying direct financial and reputational consequences. At that point, the senior technology leader could no longer operate mainly as a specialist.

The job became more integrative.

You still need technical fluency. In my experience, you need a lot of it. But the fluency is used differently. It is there to help you evaluate tradeoffs, ask sharper questions, detect weak reasoning, and understand the second-order effects of a decision. It is not there mainly so you can personally win every technical argument.

That shift is harder for experienced technologists than many people admit. Being the person with the answer is satisfying. Being the person who has to balance talent, budget, risk, timing, stakeholder needs, and organizational readiness is messier and less ego-friendly.

## The Work Became Judgment

The CTO job I know now is less about being a hero and more about making the system of decision-making better.

It is deciding whether the organization needs another architect, a stronger product-management layer, or simply fewer priorities.

It is looking at a technical roadmap and asking whether the business is actually prepared to absorb it.

It is seeing when a team is solving the technically interesting problem instead of the commercially important one.

It is knowing when to insist on rigor and when to accept debt because the timing is wrong for purity.

It is also external. Customers, executives, investors, and partners increasingly expect the senior technology leader to explain not just what the team is building, but why it matters, what it risks, and how it changes the shape of the business.

None of that is anti-technical. It is technical fluency operating at a different altitude.

## What Aspiring CTOs Usually Miss

The most common misunderstanding I see is the belief that the path to CTO is simply becoming better and better at technology until someone notices.

Technical depth matters. I would never tell an aspiring CTO to neglect it. But technical depth alone does not prepare you for the actual executive work.

What tends to be missing is practice in the adjacent disciplines that senior roles live inside:

- financial tradeoffs
- organizational design
- communication across technical and non-technical audiences
- prioritization under political and operational constraint
- talent assessment and development
- tolerance for ambiguity

You do not need an MBA-shaped personality. You do need to understand how the rest of the institution works, because sooner or later your technology decisions will only be as good as your understanding of the business context surrounding them.

The other thing people miss is that the higher you go, the less useful it is to be the person who always knows. The more useful skill is being the person who can frame the real decision clearly enough that the organization can act.

## What Has Stayed the Same

For all the change, one thing has not moved: technical credibility still matters.

The team can tell when a leader understands the work and when they are faking it. They can tell when a question is real and when it is theater. A technology executive who loses touch with the substance of the work eventually loses one of the most important sources of good judgment.

So I do not think the CTO became less technical. I think the role became less narrowly technical.

That is a meaningful difference.

The strongest technology leaders I know are not failed engineers who learned to speak business, nor business operators who happen to supervise engineers. They are people who can move between those worlds without condescension to either one.

That is the job now, or at least the version of it I recognize.
