## Script

### Cold Open

`[SLIDE 01 — black slide]`

(quiet, slightly more reflective than the other openers)

In the mid-two-thousands
I spent enough time in Tanzania
to stop noticing the thing a visitor notices first.

(beat)

Almost everyone had a cell phone.

(beat)

Not a landline at home.
Not a shared village phone.

A handset.
Usually someone else's cast-off.
Often a Nokia.
Sometimes a Razr,
if you were doing well enough
to show it.

(beat)

The network was spotty.
The electricity was spottier.
And the towers kept working anyway.
Because the business of getting a voice
from one person to another
had already won the argument there.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 02 — pull-quote: "There was no copper to retrofit. No dial-up, no DSL, no ISDN, no cable. The sequence the US and Europe lived through was not a sequence there. It was a thing that had never happened."]`

There was no copper to retrofit.

There was no dial-up.
No DSL.
No ISDN.
No cable.

The sequence the US and Europe lived through
was not a sequence there.

It was a thing
that had never happened.

`[SLIDE 03 — title card: "AI vs. DotCom, Part 5: Wireless Access and the Places That Skipped the Old Internet"]`

This is the last episode in the series.

I want to talk about who got access on what timeline.
And about whether AI is likely to reach
the people most commentators
are not currently writing about.

The question is not whether AI will help rich knowledge workers
in rich cities.

That is already happening.

The question is whether AI follows mobile's trajectory
out from the cities.

Or whether it *hardens* the gap
between places that already have expert infrastructure
and places that do not.

---

### Setup (about 60 seconds)

`[SLIDE 04 — series recap: P1 through P4]`

For anyone joining late.

In Part One I argued bubbles can build foundations.
Part Two: Amazon won retail
because it was still standing
when the checklist closed.
Part Three: streaming did not replace the disc when the internet arrived.
It replaced the disc when its checklist closed.
Part Four: the smartphone dissolved the internet
from a place you went
into an ambient layer you stand inside.

(beat)

This episode pushes on access.

I am writing this from Southeast Asia,
which lived through a version of the same compressed sequence
on a different timeline
and with different outcomes.

The series has been a single argument in five cases.

This is the final case.

### Part One: The Assumption That Dies First (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 05 — image: rural cell tower with no copper landlines anywhere in frame]`

The first thing the Tanzania experience broke
was the assumption that landlines are *easier*.

(beat)

In rich-world storytelling,
wired infrastructure is the default.
Wireless is the *upgrade*.

In places that never had the wires,
the opposite is obviously true.

Wireless is not the upgrade.

It is the only thing.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 06 — pull-quote: "An imperfect AI expert is not a downgrade from a real one. It is an upgrade from nothing."]`

This matters for the AI parallel
because the rich-world framing of AI adoption
is similarly default-loaded.

The implicit model is:
professionals in well-equipped offices
use AI as an overlay
on the knowledge work they already do.

The sequence runs
from sophisticated existing infrastructure
to AI-augmented infrastructure.

But the places where AI might do the most per dollar:
a village without a GP,
a district without a family lawyer,
a school without enough trained teachers.

They do not have that substrate to overlay onto.

For them,
an imperfect AI expert
is not a downgrade from a real one.

It is an upgrade
from nothing.

The adoption sequence is not the same sequence.

(beat)

Whether that upgrade actually arrives
and works
is a different question.

And most of this talk
is spent there.

But the sequencing assumption is worth naming first.

Because commentators keep repeating
the mistake the telecom world eventually learned from.

Projecting a rich-world infrastructure stack
onto places where the stack
was never going to be built.

---

### Part Two: The Satellite and the Rent (about 2 minutes)

`[SLIDE 07 — image: VSAT satellite dish on a rooftop, padlocked gate in foreground]`

The connectivity we had in that period
was satellite.

Not exotic.
The same physical infrastructure
that served European markets.

A two hundred and fifty-six kbps link in Europe
cost one thing.
The equivalent link in country
cost two to three times as much.

Because the regulation required the service
be delivered
through approved domestic contractors.

The satellite companies could not sell directly.
They had to sell through a local licence holder.
And the local licence holder
set the price.

(beat)

The multiplier was the visible part.

The less visible part
was *quality*.

A budget that bought you two hundred and fifty-six kbps of real throughput in Europe
bought you a two hundred and fifty-six kbps-*labelled* product
that delivered one twenty-eight.
Or in bad weather,
sixty-four.

You were not just paying more for the same service.
You were paying more
for a worse version of it.

The rent extraction was structural.

Not incidental.

(beat, careful)

I want to be careful about what this observation is and is not.

It is not a story about one country
or one government.

The pattern held across most of the region I worked in.
Variants held across much of the post-colonial world.

The deeper structure
is older than the regulators
sitting in any given office in two thousand six.

Concession economies under colonial administration
had already established the template.
A foreign operator with the technical capability.
A protected local intermediary
with the right to collect.

Independence changed the identities at the top of that structure
without dismantling the structure itself.

Layer post-independence state monopolies on telecommunications
onto that inheritance.
Layer liberalisation-era licensing regimes on top of those.

What you get
is not primarily a story about individual venality.

It is a story about what happens
when successive regimes
leave the rent-collection architecture in place
because it is easier than rebuilding it.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 08 — pull-quote: "In markets where AI is most needed but most regulated, expect a multiplier between what the technology could deliver and what end users actually pay."]`

The AI parallel here is sharper than most commentators notice.

And it is not limited to low- or middle-income countries.

Regulated AI,
in healthcare,
legal advice,
financial services,
education,
is going to route through licensed-intermediary structures
in *almost every jurisdiction*.

Rich and poor alike.

The model weights are the satellite.
The certified local operator is the domestic contractor.

The rent extraction will look similar
wherever political permission is the scarce input
and technical capability is abundant.

Whether that's a regional licensing regime in East Africa,
a state medical board in the U.S.,
or a financial-services regulator in the EU.

(flat)

This is not a complaint.
It is a *prediction*.

In markets where AI is most needed
but most regulated,
expect a multiplier
between what the technology could deliver
and what end users actually pay
for a weaker version of it.

---

### Part Three: Equipment That Would Not Translate (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 09 — image: mid-2000s laptop on a wooden table in a sparse room with a dusty manual]`

I was working with an NGO during that period.
Which is to say I was working on a shoestring.

Equipment came in from the US.
Either shipped or specified and bought locally where possible.
And arrived at the edge of an operational environment
the specifications had not anticipated.

(beat)

The unit would work out of the box.

That was not the problem.

The problem was the layer of training,
documentation,
troubleshooting,
and repair culture
the equipment had assumed was present.

And was not.

(beat)

Manuals written for a US mid-market IT department
do not survive contact with a context
where nobody has ever been that IT department.

The language was not the barrier.

The barrier was that the material assumed
a *culture of repair*
that, in that environment,
did not exist
and was not being built.

(beat, careful)

This is not a critique of the people who lived there.

It is a description of an economic reality.

If goods flow in from offshore more cheaply than they can be produced locally,
the incentive to develop repair capability disappears.

Things worked or they did not.

If they did not, you went without.

The steady-state assumption was disposability.
And disposability is not a defect of character.
It is a rational response
to the economics in front of you.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 10 — pull-quote: "All of that is cultural and institutional. It does not ship in the box with the model weights."]`

The relevance for AI
is that the most important part of deploying a technology
is rarely the technology itself.

It is the scaffolding around it.

The people who know how to prompt it well.
The workflows that turn an answer into an action.
The trust that gets built over many small uses.
The feedback loops that catch the failures early.

All of that is cultural and institutional.

It does not ship in the box
with the model weights.

It did not ship in the box
with the satellite terminals either.

(beat)

A lot of current AI pilot failure
in well-resourced organisations
is a version of the same problem.

The equipment works.
The surrounding capability does not exist.
And nobody has allocated budget or time
for the scaffolding.

In lower-resource settings,
that gap is structurally larger.

It does not mean the technology cannot help.

It means the help will depend on whether local scaffolding gets built.

And who builds it.

### Part Four: What Leapfrogging Actually Buys (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 11 — title: "What Leapfrogging Actually Buys"]`

I am not a leapfrogging purist.

Compressed development timelines are, in general, good.
Southeast Asia has lived through several of them
and come out stronger on the whole
than if it had been forced through the full Western sequence.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 12 — pull-quote: "Leapfrogging buys you the absence of legacy. The next transition gets cheaper because you do not have to unlearn anything."]`

What leapfrogging buys
is not just a better end state.

It is the development
of a particular kind of *muscle memory*.

If your first experience of communication technology
is a mobile handset rather than a rotary phone,
your default mental model of "making a call" is different.
Your relationship with the asset is different.
Your readiness for the next transition is different.

You do not have to unlearn anything.
You are not defending an investment you already made.
You do not feel obliged to justify the thing you have
by hesitating at the thing that comes next.

(beat)

It is not that skipping a step
produces a better state immediately.

It is that the *absence of legacy*
makes the next transition cheaper and faster.

Mobile-first populations moved to smartphone-first
with less friction
than populations heavily invested in feature phones.

Smartphone-first populations
will likely move to ambient and AI-augmented interaction
with less friction
than populations that are still defending
their laptop-and-browser workflows.

The muscle memory compounds.

(beat)

It also compounds in a direction you cannot fully predict.

Mobile money in Kenya
did not look like a digitised US chequing account.
Because it was not evolving out of one.

The AI equivalent is likely to look similarly unfamiliar
in places where it establishes itself first.

Less like an office worker's chatbot.

More like something embedded in agricultural advisory.
Or community health.
Or informal-sector commerce.

Shaped by constraints
that rich-world product managers
are not currently designing around.

---

### Part Five: Coverage Is Not the Same as Use (about 75 seconds)

`[SLIDE 13 — close-up: hands on a feature phone keypad, market behind]`

The most disciplined part of the current research
is the distinction between *coverage* and *meaningful use*.

GSMA's twenty twenty-five update
put the coverage gap at roughly four percent of the global population,
with a thirty-eight percent *usage gap* on top of it.

The bottleneck has migrated.

It used to be that there was no tower.

Now in most places there is a tower.

The problem is that a tower is not enough.

(beat)

Some of the most useful early mobile-enabled behaviour
in places I worked
had nothing to do with the internet.

It was direct SMS between two people
who could now coordinate a trade or a movement of goods
without either party having to travel
to find the other.

The fisheries work in India
and the grain-market work in Niger
both show the same pattern.

Reducing the cost of coordination,
even without bringing the full internet with you,
produces measurable welfare gains.

It is not glamorous.
It does not make for good pitch decks.
It is real and it compounds.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 14 — pull-quote: "The useful AI cases are smaller, more specific, and more boring than the rich-world framing suggests. They are also where the early development gains are most likely to be real."]`

The parallel question for AI
is what counts as meaningful AI use
in a context where access is thin.

Is it a polished chat interface?
Unlikely.

Is it a translated voice assistant
helping a community health worker triage a symptom?
Possibly.

Is it crop disease identification from a phone photo,
in a dialect the extension service cannot reach?
Plausibly.

The useful cases are smaller, more specific, and more boring
than the rich-world framing suggests.

They are also where the early development gains
are most likely to be real.

---

### Part Six: Where the Optimism Breaks (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 15 — title: "Where the Optimism Breaks"]`

(harder tone)

I do not want to over-sell any of this.

Every constraint that limited mobile's actual reach
applies to AI.

Often more sharply.

`[SLIDE 16 — list builds: Device cost · Electricity · Language · Regulation · Gender]`

*Device cost.*
GSMA estimates a thirty-dollar smartphone
would bring up to one point six billion more people
into the affordability range.
We are not close to that price point
for a device capable of useful on-device AI.
And cloud-dependent AI
pushes the data-cost question harder
at exactly the income levels where data cost is the binding constraint.

*Electricity.*
The IEA estimated around seven hundred and fifty million people
lacked electricity access in twenty twenty-three.
The large majority in Africa.
You cannot run an AI-assisted clinic workflow
off a phone that cannot charge reliably.
Solar and off-grid models help.
They cap the compute budget
in ways the rich-world product decisions do not anticipate.

*Language.*
The quality of most large language models
outside of English, Mandarin, and a narrow set of other global languages
remains meaningfully worse.

The populations who would benefit most from expert access
are exactly the populations
most likely to be served
in a language the model treats as an afterthought.

*Regulation.*
Markets with weak professional capacity
and strong protective licensing
are markets where AI expansion is most needed
*and* most legally complicated.

*Gender.*
Women in low- and middle-income countries
are still fourteen percent less likely
to use mobile internet than men.

Every layer of friction in AI access
lands harder on women in the same markets.

Ignoring this
does not make the aggregate numbers look better.

It makes the aggregate numbers *wrong*.

(beat)

None of this means AI cannot reach these markets.

Mobile eventually reached them,
in forms nobody fully predicted.

It means the optimistic version of the story
only holds
if serious attention is paid to the failure modes.

And those failure modes
are not the ones currently attracting product investment.

---

### Part Seven: The Contractor Pipeline (about 60 seconds)

`[SLIDE 17 — title: "The Contractor Pipeline"]`

The period I was in Tanzania
overlapped the early Afghanistan and second Gulf conflicts.

The other kind of foreigner coming through
was military and the contractor ecosystem that followed.

I spent a lot of time
in airports and on planes
with those people.

A lot of the serious remote-communications and internet technology
I was ever allowed to touch
came from that world first.
And from consumer markets second.

(beat)

This is not surprising.
The internet substrate was defence-funded.
The early networks were operated for defence purposes.
Techniques that hardened into the commercial internet
had been developed, stress-tested, and normalised
in military and intelligence contexts first.

What changed after the DotCom bubble
is that the pipeline became *bidirectional*.

Consumer scale produced hardware and software
the military then adopted.
And military investment continued to shape
what got built at the frontier.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 18 — pull-quote: "The AI pipeline is working out the same way right now."]`

The AI pipeline
is working out the same way right now.

The major labs (Anthropic among them, along with its peers)
are visibly serving US defence and intelligence customers.

The same capabilities showing up in enterprise and consumer products
are being tuned, evaluated, and hardened
under contract for classified use.

This is not a moral argument.
It is observational.

If you want to predict where AI capability will be pressed hardest,
it is worth watching where the defence contracts are going.

What is genuinely different this time
is *speed*.

The mobile internet took roughly two decades
to get from frontier research to a universal consumer substrate.
AI capability is moving through the same cycle
on a much faster clock.

That compression cuts both ways.
The capability can reach development contexts faster than mobile did.
The errors and failure modes also arrive faster.
Before the scaffolding to handle them has formed.

---

### Close: What the Series Was Arguing (about 2 minutes)

`[SLIDE 19 — title: "What This Series Was Arguing"]`

(grounded, this is the close of the whole series)

I want to end on what I am genuinely uncertain about.
Because the honest answer is uncertainty.

I do not see the low-connectivity or offline populations
benefiting meaningfully from AI yet.

The current centre of gravity is urban,
professional,
English-first,
and well-capitalised.

That is true.
It was also true of the internet in nineteen ninety-eight.
And of mobile in two thousand two.

Neither of those stayed true.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 20 — pull-quote: "The bubble is not the opposite of the revolution. The bubble is how the revolution is financed."]`

Every post in this series
has been the same argument
in a different suit.

Substrate transitions are slower, messier, and more expensive
to individual investors
than the headlines suggest.

They are also more durable,
more civilisationally consequential,
and harder to reverse
than the same headlines imply
going the other way.

The bubble is not the opposite of the revolution.

The bubble is *how the revolution is financed*.

(beat)

What matters in each case is the checklist.

The internet did not replace retail
until search, payments, logistics, trust, and habit
had all closed.

Streaming did not replace the disc
until broadband, compression, connected television,
delivery infrastructure, licensing, and subscription psychology
had all closed.

The smartphone did not dissolve the internet into the ambient layer it now is
until hardware, operating systems, app economics, carrier pricing, and social behaviour
had all closed.

Access, the case this episode is about,
is closing on a similar and partly overlapping list,
at different speeds in different places.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 21 — pull-quote: "Pandora's box is open. The genie is out of the bottle. There is no version of the next decade where AI goes away."]`

The part that matters most for AI is this.

AI is sticking around.

The internet did not get rolled back after two thousand one,
even though most of the companies did.

Mobile did not get rolled back
after the carrier consolidation waves of the late two-thousands.

Streaming did not retreat to physical media.

None of those substrates went backwards.

Because substrates do not.

Once a technology has crossed enough of its checklist
that ordinary behaviour comes to depend on it,
the behaviour pins the substrate in place.

AI is past that line in parts of software development already.

It is closing on the line
in several other professional domains.

Pandora's box is open.

The genie is out of the bottle.

There is no version of the next decade
where AI goes away.

Regardless of whether any particular frontier lab does.

(beat)

That does not mean every current AI company is a real company.
Or every current AI product is a real product.

Most of both will fail.

That is what the DotCom pattern tells you to expect.
It is what the streaming pattern tells you to expect.

What survives
is whatever sits on the correct side of the checklist
when the preconditions finish closing.
And whatever the people using the technology
continue to want
once the novelty wears off.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 22 — final card: "Be the operator still standing when the next version of normal arrives."]`

(slow, this is the last line of the series)

If I had to compress the whole series into one line,
it is this.

Substrate transitions
reward operators
who stay in the game long enough
to see the checklist close.

Not the loudest.
Not the biggest.
Not the ones who bet on the single right product.

The ones who keep building through the rocky part.

Who carry the legacy that is still paying
while they build the thing that is still becoming.

Who watch the preconditions
rather than the hype cycle.

And who understand
that the transition
is not an event.

The transition is a decade.

Sometimes two.

(beat)

AI is early in that decade now.

The work in front of us
is what the work in front of the nineteen ninety-eight internet operators was.
And what the work in front of the two thousand eight streaming operators was.

(slower)

It is to keep building.
Keep watching.

And be the person still standing
when the next version of normal
arrives.

`[SLIDE 23 — outro: jmaclabs.com / full series link / thank you]`

(warmer)

That's the series.

Five episodes, five cases, one argument.
All of them adapt essays you can read at jmaclabs dot com.
Links in the description.

If this was useful,
the series page has the others
in case you missed any.

Thanks for sticking with it.

`[END]`

## Production Notes

- The Tanzania cold open is the most personal one in the series. The temptation will be to add details. Resist; the constraint is what makes it credible.
- The colonial-history beat in Part Two is the riskiest paragraph in the whole series. Read it exactly as written. Anything off-script there will tilt out of the analytical register fast.
- Part Six (Where the Optimism Breaks) needs visible energy shift on camera. If you sound resigned the takeaway lands as defeatism rather than as an honest cost column.
- The closing line is the closer to all five talks. Mark slide 22 as the deck's final pull-quote and time the cut accordingly.
