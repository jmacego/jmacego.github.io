## Script

### Cold Open

`[SLIDE 01 — black slide]`

(quiet)

In nineteen ninety-nine
I was running a web hosting business
out of real server racks.

Selling dedicated machines to customers
who mostly reached them
from Windows desktops
sitting on furniture.

(beat)

The endpoints were *known*.

They lived in offices and bedrooms.

They plugged into the wall.

If you wanted to manage your server,
you sat down at a desk and did it.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 02 — pull-quote: "If you'd told me that within fifteen years, the majority of internet traffic would originate from handheld glass rectangles in cafes and on trains and in beds at three in the morning, I would have believed the direction and dismissed the pace."]`

(slow, reflective)

If you had told me
that within fifteen years
the majority of internet traffic
would originate from handheld glass rectangles,
in cafes and on trains and in beds at three in the morning,
from people who would never see the inside of a data centre,

I would have believed the direction.

And dismissed the pace.

(beat)

Handhelds existed in nineteen ninety-nine.
Palm Pilots.
Early WAP phones.

None of them were on a trajectory
to dissolve the distinction
between being online
and being awake.

(flat)

That is exactly what happened anyway.

`[SLIDE 03 — title card: "AI vs. DotCom, Part 4: Smartphones Made the Internet Ambient"]`

I want to talk about
how that happened,
why almost no one predicted the *shape* of it,
and what it tells us
about where AI lives next.

---

### Setup (about 60 seconds)

`[SLIDE 04 — recap of P1, P2, P3]`

If you're new:
this is part four of a five-part series
on what the DotCom era can teach us about AI.

In Part One I argued bubbles can build foundations.
In Part Two: the rewiring of retail took two decades.
In Part Three: streaming did not replace the disc when the internet arrived.
It replaced the disc when the *checklist* closed.

(beat)

This part makes the next claim.

The biggest change the internet delivered
was not any particular product.

It was the transition
from *destination*
to *substrate*.

That happened through the smartphone.
Through a period that was considerably rockier
than most retrospectives admit.

If streaming was the application
the ambient internet would eventually carry,
the smartphone was what made the internet ambient
in the first place.

One layer further down.
One stage earlier in the sequence.

---

### Part One: The Ladder That Was a Curve (about 2 minutes)

`[SLIDE 05 — Pew curve: 35% in 2011 → 91% in 2025]`

In May twenty eleven,
when Pew Research ran its first smartphone ownership survey,
thirty-five percent of U.S. adults owned one.

By twenty twenty-five
that figure was ninety-one percent.

Globally, four point six billion people were using mobile internet
on their own device by the end of twenty twenty-three.
The Ericsson Mobility Report records
five point six billion new smartphone subscriptions
added between twenty eleven and twenty twenty.
And roughly *three hundred times*
more mobile data traffic
over the same period.

(beat)

Those are not numbers that describe a gradual rollout.

They describe an S-curve
steeper than broadband
and denser than the PC.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 06 — visual: ladder of milestones overlaid on a contested-years jagged curve]`

What looks in hindsight like a ladder,
iPhone two thousand seven,
App Store two thousand eight,
Android two thousand eight,
4G twenty ten,
mobile-majority by twenty fifteen,

was in real time
a series of *contested years*
where no one knew yet
which phones would matter,
which platforms would survive,
or which app categories would hold value.

The direction was visible.

The pace and the shape were not.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 07 — pull-quote: "Direction visible. Pace and shape not. That is the gap during which positioning matters most."]`

That distinction matters.

Because it is the *single thing*
most AI coverage in twenty twenty-six
is getting wrong.

The direction of ambient, workflow-integrated AI
is visible to almost anyone paying attention.

The pace and the specific shape it will take
are not.

(beat)

The twenty twenty-four NBER paper from Bick, Blandin, and Deming
found roughly forty percent of U.S. adults
aged eighteen to sixty-four
were already using generative AI by late twenty twenty-four.

While the Census Bureau's firm-level surveys
showed business adoption
still in the *single digits*.

That gap
between individual usage and firm-level transformation
is the exact gap that existed
between smartphone ownership
and smartphone-native business operations
in twenty twelve.

That is the gap during which positioning matters most.

---

### Part Two: The Storefront Was Not the Business (about 2 minutes)

`[SLIDE 08 — App Store launch screen, 500 apps]`

Apple launched the App Store in July two thousand eight
with five hundred applications.

By twenty twenty-three,
the app economy was a multi-trillion-dollar distribution layer
that had reshaped retail,
payments,
transportation,
dating,
healthcare booking,
and the internal tooling
of almost every large business
that had a workforce interacting with the outside world.

(beat)

The App Store was not the business.

It was the substrate
on which businesses that could not have existed ten years earlier
grew to sizes that would have been absurd to forecast in two thousand eight.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 09 — logo grid: Uber, Instagram, WhatsApp, Snapchat, Venmo, Airbnb, DoorDash, Robinhood, Duolingo]`

Uber incorporated in two thousand nine
and ran its first rides in San Francisco in twenty ten.
Instagram launched in October twenty ten.
WhatsApp.
Snapchat.
Venmo.
Airbnb.
DoorDash.
Robinhood.
Duolingo.

Most of the consumer surface of modern financial services.

All built on the assumption
of a pocket-sized computer
with a camera,
a location sensor,
a payment credential,
and a persistent connection.

None of those assumptions held in two thousand six.

By twenty thirteen, all of them did.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 10 — pull-quote: "Smartphones did not just create new companies. They relocated where value accrued."]`

The durable lesson of the app era
is not that smartphones created new companies.

It is that they *relocated* where value accrued.

The storefront,
the App Store itself,
the handset,
the operating system,
captured a *fraction* of the economic surplus.

The rest accrued
to whoever built businesses
that presupposed smartphone ubiquity
as a starting condition,
rather than a feature.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 11 — split: "Storefronts (today): ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini" / "Substrate businesses (emerging)"]`

The AI equivalent
is already visible if you watch for it.

ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and the other chat interfaces
are storefronts.

They are real, they are valuable,
and they are *not the durable business*.

The durable businesses
are being built by companies whose products
*presuppose* capable AI as a starting condition.

Coding environments where agentic assistance
is load-bearing rather than optional.
Medical imaging pipelines where model-assisted reading is the default.
Legal research platforms where the human attorney
is shepherding AI-generated analysis
rather than producing it from scratch.

Those products will not be called "AI products."

They will be called *products*.

The way we stopped calling Uber a mobile-app company
and started calling it a transportation company.

(beat)

(drier)

Trying to pick the winning foundation model
is the twenty twenty-six equivalent
of trying to pick the winning handset
in two thousand eight.

The reliable bet
was not on the handset.

It was on businesses that would benefit
*regardless* of which handset won.

---

### Part Three: From Pocket to Layer (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 12 — title: "From Pocket to Layer"]`

The cleanest analytical move available for this talk
is also the hardest one to articulate
without sounding like a TED talk.

(self-aware grin)

I'll try anyway.

The smartphone's durable consequence
was *not portability*.

Portability was the surface feature.

The durable consequence was *ambient presence*.

(beat)

In two thousand five,
using the internet required intentional engagement.

You sat down.
You opened a browser.
You went somewhere.

The internet was a place with edges.
Between trips to that place,
you were offline.

Offline was a *default condition*
most of the time.

(beat)

By twenty fifteen,
offline had become a state
you actively had to *produce*.

Your pocket was online by default.
Your wrist might also be online.
Your car increasingly was.
Notifications arrived whether you solicited them or not.
Location was continuously legible.
Payments were a gesture.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 13 — pull-quote: "The internet had not gotten bigger. It had gotten ambient."]`

The internet had not gotten bigger.

It had gotten *ambient*.

It was no longer something you used.

It was something you *inhabited*.

That is the substrate shift.

And most of the people who built the best businesses of the twenty-tens
understood it implicitly,
even when they did not articulate it.

(beat, slowing)

Current AI, in twenty twenty-six,
is mostly still a place you *go*.

You open a tab.
You switch out of the tool you were working in.
You paste context back and forth across the boundary.

That is the two thousand eight version of the transition.

It is real. It is useful.
It is not the final form.

The final form,
the thing that will actually change behaviour and markets,
is AI as a capability
present in the environment where work already happens.
Available without the ceremony of switching.
Context-aware without being told.

Invisible enough
that people stop noticing
it is there.

---

### Part Four: The Incumbents Mispriced It (about 2 minutes)

`[SLIDE 14 — Nokia, BlackBerry, Palm, Microsoft Mobile logos with peak share %]`

The smartphone transition was not a smooth march toward ambient.

It was a period of sustained capital destruction
and operator error
that is considerably uglier in the record
than the hindsight version suggests.

(beat)

Nokia held roughly forty percent of the global mobile phone market
in two thousand seven.
By twenty thirteen
it had sold its handset business to Microsoft
at a price that reflected
how thoroughly the franchise had been destroyed.

BlackBerry peaked at nearly fifty percent U.S. smartphone share
in two thousand nine.
And fell below one percent
within six years.

Palm was sold to HP in twenty ten and shut down by twenty eleven.

Microsoft's own mobile effort
was effectively abandoned.

(beat, deliberate)

`[SLIDE 15 — pull-quote: "None of them missed mobile. They saw mobile clearly. They were mispriced."]`

None of them missed mobile.

They saw mobile clearly.

Nokia had a better manufacturing operation than Apple.
BlackBerry had a better keyboard, better security, better battery life.
Microsoft had every possible software advantage.

They were not blind.

They were *mispriced*.

Each of them was running a business
whose cost structure,
organisational assumptions,
and internal incentives
had been calibrated for a *different substrate*.

When the substrate changed,
the calibration could not change fast enough.

The moat became the weight.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 16 — title: "The current AI equivalent is legacy enterprise SaaS"]`

The current AI equivalent
is already visible.

It is legacy enterprise SaaS.

A long tail of vendors
with entrenched positions,
bloated feature sets,
and unsustainable prices,
is being undercut by AI-fronted competitors
on price and speed at once.

Some of that is deserved.
A SaaS product that charges enterprise prices
for a mediocre workflow
that a subsidised AI wrapper can replicate at a lower price
is a product the market is correctly *repricing*.

Some of it is subsidy-driven and temporary.
Because the AI wrappers doing the damage
are often operating on inference economics
that do not yet reflect the real cost.

But the subsidy being temporary
does not save the incumbents
whose cost structure depended on the old substrate continuing.

That is the Nokia pattern.
They are not blind.
They are *calibrated*
for a substrate that is shifting.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 17 — hyperscaler capex chart: MSFT, GOOG, META, AMZN totalling ~$600B in 2026 guidance]`

The capital going into foundation model training
and inference capacity
is historically large.

Microsoft.
Alphabet.
Meta.
Amazon.

Combined, the four hyperscalers
are guiding to close to *six hundred billion dollars*
of twenty twenty-six capex.

Some meaningful fraction of that
will prove to have been wrong
in the specifics of where, when, and for which model generation.
Even if the substrate bet is directionally correct.

The dark fibre comparison
this series has used before
applies here too.

The infrastructure outlasts the investors.

The investors often do not.

### Part Five: What Was Actually Lost (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 18 — title: "What Was Actually Lost"]`

(more grounded, harder tone)

Any honest assessment of the ambient internet
has to include what it cost.

Because the AI transition
will involve similar tradeoffs.
And the rhetorical habit of pretending those tradeoffs do not exist
is itself part of the problem.

(beat)

The dedicated camera industry collapsed.
Ninety-four percent drop in shipments
between twenty ten and twenty twenty-three.
Paper maps. Standalone GPS units. Portable music players. Pocket calculators. Low-end gaming handhelds.
Same curve.

That is category destruction.
For the people who worked in those industries
it was not abstract.

(beat)

Work-life boundary erosion.
Newspapers collapsed.
Local advertising markets hollowed out.
Political discourse restructured around formats that reward intensity over duration.
Adolescent mental health indicators worsened materially after twenty twelve
in ways that track smartphone and social media adoption closely.

(beat, careful)

None of that invalidates the underlying technology.

All of it is part of the honest cost column.

Ambient
means pervasive
means unavoidable
means
*changes everyone*,
including people who did not choose the change.

(beat)

The AI parallel is obvious
and deserves to be named
rather than gestured at.

Dependency on systems whose internals are not auditable.
Homogenisation of output
when large populations draft through the same models.
Erosion of the low-stakes friction
that used to force humans to develop their own judgment.
Displacement of entry-level work
that functioned as a training ground for mid-career competence.

None of these are hypothetical.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 19 — two-cost callout: "Subsidy" and "AI Tech Debt"]`

Two costs more particular to the current AI moment
also deserve naming.

*Subsidy.*

A lot of what looks like AI disruption in twenty twenty-six
is not AI disruption.

It is *subsidised AI inference*
out-pricing unsubsidised software.

Foundation-model providers are funding inference at prices
that do not reflect its real cost.
Venture capital is underwriting the rest.

That is not a stable configuration.

When the subsidy ends,
and it will,
the clearing price is going to look
quite different
from what the current price suggests.

*AI tech debt.*

AI can build the first ninety percent of a product
faster than most teams can type.

If that ninety percent was vibe-coded
without strong engineering oversight,
the remaining ten percent
will take a hundred and ten percent
of the effort it would have taken
to build the thing properly in the first place.

Teams are already hitting that wall.

(beat)

That is not a reason to stop using AI for engineering.

It is a reason to notice
that engineering judgment
has become more valuable.
Not less.

Precisely because the cost of producing
a plausible-looking wrong answer
has dropped to near zero.

### Part Six: AI's Ambient Form (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 20 — image: developer at laptop, AI assistance woven into editor inline]`

(shift to forward-looking)

The chat window is to AI
what the Palm Pilot was to mobile.

A real product solving real friction.
Training a generation of users on what the capability feels like.
Building a base
that the next form will inherit.

It is not the form that actually reshapes work.

The form that reshapes work
is the one that stops being a thing you go to.

(beat)

What that form looks like, concretely,
is already visible
in the parts of the economy
where AI has the deepest penetration.

In modern coding environments,
AI assistance is in the editor.
Not in a tab.

It sees the repository.
It sees the file you are editing.
The tests you have run.
The git history around the change.

It offers completions as you type.
Not after you ask.

Some portion of the work
has already become AI-assisted
in a way that would be awkward to roll back.
Not because the model is spectacular.
Because the *integration is structural*.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 21 — pull-quote: "None of those changes will feel like an AI rollout. They will feel like the tool got better."]`

That same pattern,
when it arrives elsewhere,
will not announce itself.

Your CRM will start proposing next-best-actions
that make the obvious next action five seconds faster.
And then three months later
your reps will not know how to operate without them.

Your document editor will start surfacing context and references
you used to have to go look up.
And then the step of going to look things up
will feel archaic.

Your imaging system at the hospital
will present prior-scan comparisons
and literature-derived differentials
alongside the scan itself.
And the radiologist will still decide.
But the decision will be made
inside a richer context
than it was in twenty twenty-four.

(beat, land it)

None of those changes
will feel like an AI rollout.

They will feel
like the tool got better.

That is what ambient looks like.

### Close: Position Through the Rocky Part (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 22 — title: "How to Position Through the Rocky Part"]`

(brisk, summary)

Six habits.
Same six I gave you last episode.
This time backed by mobile-era evidence rather than streaming-era evidence.

`[SLIDE 23 — list reveals: Stop betting on a model · Build for the substrate · Build the harness · Position your career toward judgment · Accept the rocky part · Build what needs to be built]`

*Stop betting on which specific model wins.*
That is two thousand eight's
betting on a handset.

*Build for the substrate instead.*
Ask what your product, your workflow, or your role looks like
when ambient AI is an *environmental condition*
rather than a feature.

If the answer is
"exactly the same, but with an AI chat widget bolted to the side,"
you are building the equivalent
of a two thousand nine mobile-responsive website
for a business that is about to be disintermediated
by an app-native competitor.

*Build the harness, not the token.*
Tooling, evaluation, retrieval,
agent orchestration,
workflow integration,
domain-specific knowledge encoded into these layers.
That is where compounding advantage lives.

*Position your career toward roles
where ambient AI raises your ceiling
rather than threatens your floor.*
Judgment, taste, relationship, orchestration, synthesis.
Applied inside a substrate
that makes your reach larger.
That is not a trick.
It is a general pattern.

*Accept the rocky part.*
Some data centre builds will not pencil out.
Some model providers will be acquired
at fractions of their last round.
Some product categories that look inevitable today
will turn out to have been subsidised novelties.

That was also true of mobile.
It did not prevent mobile from becoming the substrate
we now take for granted.

*Build what needs to be built.*
The most frequent error during a substrate transition
is to freeze.
Wait for the dust to settle.
Re-enter when the shape is clearer.

(flat)

The shape is never clearer.

The shape is obvious in retrospect
and negotiable in the present.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 24 — closing card]`

(slow, close)

The internet became ambient
because enough people kept building
through a decade of expensive mistakes.

AI will become ambient,
or not,
on a similar schedule
and by a similar mechanism.

Whether you are standing on the right side of the transition
ten years from now
depends less
on predicting the winners
and more
on what you choose to build now,
in the rocky part,
while the shape is still being worked out.

`[SLIDE 25 — outro: jmaclabs.com / next: Wireless Access]`

(warmer)

Last episode in the series:
*Wireless Access and the Places That Skipped the Old Internet.*
A view from places where the rich-world infrastructure stack never existed.
And what that says about who AI actually reaches.

Thanks for watching.

`[END]`
