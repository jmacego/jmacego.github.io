## Script

### Cold Open

`[SLIDE 01 — black slide]`

(quiet)

Netflix launched in April nineteen ninety-eight.

(beat)

That same month
I was running PowerSurge,
deploying dedicated servers,
trying to make DNS manageable
for people who should not have to love raw BIND configuration.

We were on the same internet.

We were building entirely different things.

(beat)

They were betting the network was good enough
to manage the logistics of physical media.
I was betting the network was good enough
to run a hosting business.

We were *both* right.
Both bets worked. For a while.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 02 — pull-quote: "Neither of us was betting the network was good enough to deliver video. Because in April 1998, it was not."]`

(slowly)

Neither of us
was betting the network was good enough
to deliver video.

Because in April nineteen ninety-eight,

it was not.

`[SLIDE 03 — title card: "AI vs. DotCom, Part 3: From DVD Mail to Streaming"]`

I want to talk about
how it eventually became good enough.

Because that twelve-year stretch
from "video doesn't work on the internet"
to "every screen in your house is a streaming endpoint"
is the cleanest historical parallel available
for what AI is trying to become *right now*.

And most of the coverage
is making the exact mistake
that everyone made about streaming
the first time around.

---

### Setup (about 60 seconds)

`[SLIDE 04 — recap: P1 / P2 → P3]`

Quick context.

In Part One I argued bubbles can build foundations.
In Part Two I argued the rewiring of retail
took two decades
and required a checklist of conditions
to align.

This episode pushes that further.

Streaming is the next case in that pattern.
It is also the cleanest analogue I have
for what AI is trying to do.
Because it was a *substrate transition*,
running on top of bubble-era infrastructure,
that took a full decade longer
than anyone at the time expected.

(beat)

The pipes that eventually carried Netflix to your TV
were laid during the DotCom overbuild
*I watched from the inside*.

Telecoms raised enormous capital in nineteen ninety-nine and two thousand
against revenue forecasts that didn't materialise for a decade,
went bankrupt,
and left transport capacity
that later buyers acquired
at cents on the dollar.

The bubble did not pay for streaming directly.

It paid for the pipes streaming needed.
Years before anyone knew what the pipes were for.

That's the pattern this whole series keeps coming back to.

### Part One: The Red Envelope Was the Prototype (about 2 minutes)

`[SLIDE 05 — image: red Netflix DVD mailer beside a laptop with a queue]`

Netflix spent its first nine years
as a postal service.

(drier)

By April two thousand seven
it had six point eight million subscribers.
That business was almost entirely physical.

A large catalogue.
A queue system.
No late fees.
A fulfilment network that could reach most U.S. subscribers
with one-business-day disc delivery.

What the internet had contributed
was not video delivery.

It made the *logistics* of physical media
dramatically better
than a rental store ever could.

You browsed online.
You managed a queue.
You received mail.
You returned mail.

The discovery and fulfilment were digital.
The content moved on polycarbonate.

(beat)

Streaming launched quietly inside that business
in January two thousand seven.
As a *bonus feature*.

Netflix's own twenty oh nine filings
still described the DVD-and-streaming bundle
as the core product.
Roughly two million discs shipped daily.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 06 — pull-quote: "The red envelope was not a primitive version of streaming. It was a different product that solved a different problem."]`

(this is the key reframe of the whole talk)

That sequencing is undervalued
in most retrospective accounts.

The internet did not replace physical video in one move.

It first made physical video radically more convenient,
built a subscriber base and a recommendation engine on that convenience,
and *only later*
became reliable enough to replace the disc itself.

The red envelope was *not* a primitive version of streaming.
It was a different product
that solved a different problem.

And its success
built the base from which streaming grew.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 07 — bold statement card: "AI is in the red envelope era right now."]`

(land it)

AI is in the red envelope era
right now.

(beat)

Current AI tools are genuinely useful.
They solve real friction.
For a meaningful range of tasks
they are clearly better than what came before.

But the chat interface.
The browser tab you switch to.
The API you call separately
from everything else you're doing.

(beat)

These
are the queue management
and the postal fulfilment
of the AI story.

The underlying capability is real.
The delivery mechanism is *provisional*.

The internet managed disc logistics in two thousand seven
not because that was the ideal answer.
But because it was the best answer available
given the infrastructure.

Current AI interfaces are roughly the same.

The best available answer
given current infrastructure.

And almost certainly *not the final form*.

### Part Two: What Had to Become True (about 3 minutes)

`[SLIDE 08 — title: "What Had to Become True"]`

Broadband is the explanation most people reach for
when explaining why streaming took until around twenty ten
to become the dominant delivery model.

Broadband is not wrong.
It is incomplete to the point of misleading.

Several things had to become true
at roughly the same time.

`[SLIDE 09 — checklist build: 1) Compression / adaptive delivery 2) Connected TV 3) Delivery infrastructure 4) Licensing 5) Subscription psychology]`

(work through the list, brisk pace)

*Compression and adaptive delivery had to improve.*
H two six four encoding raised the quality ceiling at a given bitrate.
HTTP-based adaptive streaming
made video resilient on real consumer connections.
These were not consumer-visible features.
They were infrastructure changes
that made the consumer experience possible.

(beat)

*The television had to become internet-connected.*

This is the step most accounts treat as incidental.

It was the *hinge*.

Connected-TV penetration was twenty-four percent in twenty ten,
thirty-eight percent in twenty twelve,
forty-nine percent in twenty fourteen.
By twenty fourteen,
nearly eighty percent of Netflix streaming
was happening on a TV set.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 10 — pull-quote: "The behaviour did not change because the technology got better in the abstract. It changed because the technology reached the right room."]`

Before that threshold,
streaming was something you did at a desk.

After it,
streaming was what you did on the couch.

The behaviour did not change
because the technology got better in the abstract.

It changed
because the technology reached
the right room.

(beat)

Chromecast shipped in July twenty thirteen.
Fire TV in April twenty fourteen.

For thirty-five dollars
you could turn any reasonably recent TV
into a streaming endpoint.

The connected-TV hinge
did not close when the first smart TVs shipped.
It closed when the cheapest path
from an ordinary television to a streaming service
dropped below the cost of a single month of cable.

(lean in)

The AI equivalent is worth watching for.

The device,
the browser extension,
the editing surface
that collapses the friction
of getting capable AI
into the tool where someone *already works*.

That has not arrived yet.

When it does, the usage curve will not be subtle.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 11 — quick pass: delivery infra · licensing · subscription psychology]`

The other items closed too.

Netflix launched Open Connect in October twenty twelve,
placing servers directly inside ISPs.
By spring twenty eleven, Netflix was thirty percent
of North American peak downstream traffic.

You cannot serve that share
from a central cloud.
The network had to be re-engineered
around the demand.

Rights licensing had to evolve.
A DVD could be purchased and owned.
A stream could be recalled overnight.
The Starz deal expiring in early twenty twelve
illustrated what that leverage meant in practice.

Then the psychology of pricing.
The seven ninety-nine streaming-only plan in November twenty ten
reframed the question.
Not DVDs with a bonus feature.
Unlimited instant access for the price of a modest monthly utility.
By January twenty eleven
more than a third of new subscribers
were choosing streaming-only.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 12 — checklist with AI translation column]`

Now run the same checklist against AI.

*Reliable inference at reasonable cost*
is the broadband equivalent.
At the commodity layer, costs are falling.
At the frontier, they are not.

*Workflow integration* is the connected TV.
AI is still largely a separate thing you go to.
The behavioural shift comes
when it is embedded
in the IDE,
the document editor,
the CRM,
the hospital records system.

*Resilience under real conditions*
is the adaptive streaming equivalent.
AI tools that perform well on benchmarks
and degrade badly at the edges of real tasks
are still in the pre-H two six four era.

*Regulatory and trust frameworks*
are the rights licensing.
There is no settled model
for IP in training data,
liability for output,
or sector-specific constraints in medicine and law.

(beat, drier)

Tracking broadband adoption
was the wrong single variable
for predicting when streaming would win.

Tracking model quality
is probably the wrong single variable
for predicting when AI becomes embedded behaviour.

The checklist matters more
than any single item on it.

### Part Three: Blockbuster Tried (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 13 — image: closing-era Blockbuster store]`

Blockbuster had four thousand eight hundred and fifty-five U.S. stores
as of January two thousand eight.

It also had a by-mail service.
Digital downloading partnerships.
On-demand deals with device manufacturers.
A programme that let mail subscribers exchange discs in store.

It was not doing nothing.

(flat)

It was doing things from the wrong cost base.

(beat)

The store network that had been a competitive moat
became a fixed liability when the traffic moved online.

The debt load,
over nine hundred million dollars at the time of bankruptcy,
was carried by a business
that needed sustained store-level revenue
to service it.

Netflix was building fulfilment infrastructure
*and* investing in streaming,
without a legacy estate consuming the margin.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 14 — pull-quote: "What we misjudged was how quickly to move there."]`

Blockbuster's shareholder communications
include a line worth keeping.

*What we misjudged
was how quickly to move there.*

(beat)

They saw the direction.
They misjudged the pace.
And they misunderstood
what their cost structure could absorb.

That failure pattern recurs reliably.

The incumbent is not usually blind.

It is usually *trapped*.

Trapped by infrastructure that was a moat yesterday
and is overhead today.

Trapped by a customer base
that still wants the old thing,
until suddenly it does not.

Trapped by financial commitments
made to sustain the old model
while trying to fund the new one.

(beat)

The parallel for AI is not abstract.

A professional services firm
whose billing model depends on junior staff hours.
A consulting practice
built on research that capable AI can now do in minutes.
A law firm whose leverage ratio
requires first-year associates doing document review.

These are organisations
approaching AI from something close to Blockbuster's position.

Not because they are ignorant of the direction.

Because their cost structure
constrains how fast they can actually move.
Regardless of what they put on slides.

### Part Four: From Schedule to Control (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 15 — visual: clock face dissolving into pause/play controls]`

The consumer behaviour shift
that made streaming irreversible
was not primarily about convenience.

It was about a different relationship with *time*.

(beat)

Store rental had a return window.
DVD-by-mail had a queue cycle measured in days.
Early streaming still carried a mental frame
of watching as something you *planned*.

What changed between roughly twenty ten and twenty thirteen
was that watching something on your own schedule,
pausing across evenings,
starting the next episode immediately because it was already there,
stopped feeling like indulgence
and started feeling like *normal*.

By twenty thirteen,
eighty-eight percent of Netflix users
reported watching three or more episodes of the same show
in a single day.

Netflix's decision to release all thirteen episodes of *House of Cards* at once
in February twenty thirteen
was not only a marketing choice.

It was an argument
that control of schedule
was itself a product feature.

The algorithm stopped being a discovery tool
and started being a relationship.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 16 — pull-quote: "The shift from schedule to control is the behavioural precedent most relevant to AI."]`

The shift from schedule to control
is the behavioural precedent most relevant to AI.

Most AI usage today is *transactional* and *deliberate*.

You identify a need.
You switch to an AI tool.
You make a request.
You take the output back to whatever you were doing.

That is roughly equivalent
to using a DVD rental store in nineteen ninety-nine.

Functional.
Somewhat better than the alternative.
But requiring a mode switch
and a conscious decision
each time.

The embedded version is different.

When AI is present in the tool you're already using.
When it can see the context of your work
without being explicitly fed it.
When suggestions appear
without a trip to a separate interface.

The relationship shifts
from tool to environment.

That is the connected TV moment.
That is the shift from schedule to control.

Parts of software development are approaching that threshold now.
Most professional domains are not there yet.

### Part Five: The Originals Problem (about 90 seconds)

`[SLIDE 17 — title: "The Originals Problem"]`

Streaming had a structural fragility
that the DVD business did not.

A disc could be purchased under the first-sale doctrine,
owned,
monetised across many rentals
without further payment to the studio.

A stream required a licence.
And that licence could be withdrawn.

When the Starz deal expired in early twenty twelve,
content disappeared overnight.

A streaming service dependent entirely on third-party rights
was always one negotiation away
from a depleted library.

(beat)

Original programming was not primarily a prestige move.

It was a response to *structural vulnerability*.

Content Netflix created
was content Netflix *owned*.

(beat, slightly personal)

I came out of media recently enough
to have watched the next chapter up close.

Apple launched TV+ in November twenty nineteen
with originals from day one.
Amazon closed its acquisition of MGM in March twenty twenty-two
for eight point five billion dollars.

The platforms had stopped being tenants in someone else's catalogue
and started being the studios themselves.

I was at Disney
when Disney+ launched in November twenty nineteen.
What the outside saw
was a service going from zero to tens of millions of subscribers
faster than any launch in the history of media.

What the inside saw
was every content team,
every production pipeline,
every post house,
every localisation team
absorbing the weight of a subscription product
that demanded continuous new material
to justify its price.

Then the pandemic hit five months later
and compressed the timeline further.

It is worth naming
because the scale-up story
in a substrate transition
is not free.

Somebody pays for it
in sustained pressure,
long before the market decides whether the bet worked.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 18 — pull-quote: "Strategic depth means owning something. Anything that is not a rewrapped call to somebody else's API."]`

The AI parallel is not theoretical.

Anthropic, OpenAI, and their peers
are increasingly not just providing capability
but building the products on top of it.

The rumoured sixty-billion-dollar Cursor acquisition,
if it lands,
is primarily a *talent buyout*
dressed as an IP transaction.

These are not ordinary talent acquisitions
routed through recruiting.

They are companies paying *studio-acquisition prices*
to secure the people who can build the next layer up.

Organisations currently renting AI capability
from foundation-model providers
are in the Starz-era streaming-service position.

Real capability,
on terms someone else controls.

The model providers, meanwhile,
are doing what Netflix did.
Moving upstream into the product layer.

Strategic depth, in this era,
means owning *something*.

Data.
Workflow.
Relationship.
Brand.

Anything
that is not a rewrapped call
to somebody else's API.

### Part Six: The Buffering Era (about 75 seconds)

`[SLIDE 19 — image: pixelated low-res streaming buffering icon overlaid on a hallucinated AI text fragment]`

In two thousand seven and two thousand eight,
streaming worked badly often enough
that using it for anything important
felt unwise.

The picture would drop to a low resolution.
The spinner would appear mid-scene.
Some evenings it simply did not work.

People used it anyway.
Because imperfect streaming was still genuinely useful
for a lot of content.
They kept the DVD subscription alongside it.
Because the disc still won on reliability
for anything that mattered.

(beat, slow)

This is AI in twenty twenty-six,
rendered almost exactly.

Hallucinations are frequent enough to be normal.
Context limitations cause real problems
at the edges of real tasks.
The model confidently produces something wrong
often enough
that handing output to anyone without checking
carries real risk.

People use it anyway.
Because it is genuinely useful for a wide range of work.
They keep the human process running alongside.
Because for anything that matters,
the human still wins on reliability.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 20 — pull-quote: "The buffering era ends. It always does."]`

The buffering era ends.

It always does.

The question it leaves behind
is which applications
become *embedded habits*
before the buffering ends.

And which ones
are still waiting for a reliability threshold
they have not yet hit.

### Close: How to Ride a Substrate Transition (about 2 minutes)

`[SLIDE 21 — title: "How to Ride a Substrate Transition"]`

People ask me what to actually do about AI.
In a career.
Or a business.
Right now.

The streaming transition is the cleanest historical parallel I have.
Here is the short list,
in the order I'd use it.

(crisp, build through the list)

`[SLIDE 22 — list builds: 1) Watch the checklist, not the hype cycle 2) Carry the legacy that's still paying 3) Don't commit to the Blockbuster position 4) Build the harness, not the hero bet 5) Protect optionality 6) Build through the rocky part]`

*Watch the checklist, not the hype cycle.*
The question that matters for your category
is which preconditions are still missing for embedded AI behaviour.
For software development,
most of the checklist has already closed.
For medicine, law, and education,
reliability and regulation are still open
and the timeline is genuinely uncertain.

*Carry the legacy that is still paying.*
DVD-by-mail generated meaningful profit for Netflix
for years after streaming became the narrative.
Plenty of DotCom-era businesses still generate real profit today
on infrastructure that was written off in two thousand one.
The same will be true of a lot of today's pre-AI workflows.

*Do not commit to the Blockbuster position.*
If your cost structure depends on the old substrate continuing,
and the preconditions for the new one are closing,
you have less time than you think.

*Build the harness, not the hero bet.*
Betting on which foundation model wins
is the wrong-shaped risk.
Building the harness that works with whichever model wins
is the right-shaped one.

*Protect optionality.*
In two thousand seven the right bet was not
to abandon DVDs for streaming.
It was to run both.
Build subscribers on the one that paid now.
Be ready to shift weight when the conditions closed.

*Build through the rocky part.*
The people who came through DotCom with businesses intact
kept building through the interval where direction was obvious
and reliability was not.
They didn't swing for the fences.
They compounded.

(beat)

`[SLIDE 23 — pull-quote: "Substrate transitions reward durability over heroics."]`

If I had to compress everything in this series into one line,
it would be the last of those.

Substrate transitions reward durability over heroics.

Stay in the game.
Watch the preconditions.
Move when they close.

Do not shoot for the moon.
Shoot for being the operator still standing
when the next version of normal arrives.

(longer beat)

`[SLIDE 24 — closing card]`

(slow, deliberate)

The internet did not change how people watch things
when streaming launched.

It changed how people watch things
when the stream
reliably reached the couch.

The question worth asking now
is what AI's couch is.

And how far away
it actually is.

`[SLIDE 25 — outro: jmaclabs.com / next: Smartphones Made the Internet Ambient]`

(warmer)

Next in the series:
*Smartphones Made the Internet Ambient.*
Why the device, not the website,
is the structural legacy
of the DotCom era.
And what it predicts
about where AI lives next.

Thanks for watching.

`[END]`
