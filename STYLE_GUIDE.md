# John MacDonald — Editorial Style Guide

## Purpose

This guide defines the house voice for John MacDonald's professional site: essays and occasional instructional pieces about leadership, organizations, technology, careers, and systems thinking.

The writing should sound like John MacDonald: a people-focused technology leader with deep technical roots, broad life experience, and real responsibility for outcomes. Not startup hype. Not generic executive content. Not motivational LinkedIn prose. Not detached technical documentation.

## Core Identity

The reader should hear a seasoned operator and leader who has spent decades close to systems, teams, outages, tradeoffs, mentoring, and organizational design.

The voice is:
- experienced
- direct
- reflective
- calm
- specific
- evidence-aware

The site is professional, but not stiff. Personal material is welcome when it sharpens judgment, leadership, resilience, or career perspective. Self-deprecation is good. Oversharing is not.

## Default Mode

The default post is an essay, not a tips list.

Most pieces should move from lived experience to analysis to a broader lesson. They should feel like a smart practitioner thinking in public, not a content marketer packaging advice.

Instructional posts are welcome, but they should still sound like John working through a real problem with constraints, tradeoffs, and failure modes. The goal is judgment, not checklist theater.

Useful structures:
- situation -> tension -> lesson
- observation -> analysis -> implication
- story -> pattern -> broader leadership point

## Narrative Stance

Write in the first person singular by default. Use "we" only when the experience or conclusion was genuinely shared.

Write from responsibility, not performance. The underlying stance is:

> This is what I saw. This is what I thought. This is what was actually happening. This is what I understand now.

Do not write like a guru above the work. Write like someone who has had to make decisions, live with consequences, and revise his views.

The lesson should emerge from the situation. Do not announce a grand universal truth in paragraph one and then backfill examples.

## Subject Focus

This site works best at the intersection of:
- leadership
- organizational design
- engineering and operations
- mentorship and career development
- systems thinking
- adaptation across life and career stages

Technology should remain present even when a post is not deeply technical. John is not a generic leadership writer who happens to have worked in tech. His authority comes partly from technical depth and partly from years of leading people and systems under real constraints.

Strong recurring themes:
- structure shapes behavior
- reliability is cultural before it is technical
- speed comes from clarity, not chaos
- customer needs and operational reality both matter
- craft matters at scale
- patterns repeat across domains
- judgment is contextual, not ideological

## Research Standard

Experience is the starting point, not the whole argument.

For any post making broader claims about leadership, organizations, behavior, careers, or technology, research should be significant from the beginning. John's instincts should be supported, refined, or challenged by serious outside material.

Use research for two reasons:
1. To strengthen sound conclusions.
2. To discover where John may be wrong, incomplete, or overgeneralizing.

Prefer:
- primary research
- reputable industry studies
- strong reporting
- serious management thinkers
- direct operational evidence where available

Avoid:
- trend fluff
- empty executive surveys
- quote collections from famous CEOs
- business-book name-dropping with no analytical value

Do not let citations take over the piece. The ideal effect is: this came from lived experience, and the wider evidence either supports it or forced a correction.

Use honest scope. Phrases like "in my experience," "in that environment," "for that kind of team," and "at that stage" are strengths when they keep the argument true.

## Further Reading

Published posts should usually end with a short `Further Reading` section containing 2 to 4 genuinely useful follow-ups.

This section is for the reader, not for proof of homework. Include the best next sources, not the largest pile. Skip it only when the post is so personal or so brief that it would feel artificial.

## Prose Standard

The prose should be clean, muscular, and controlled. Edited, but not sterilized.

Prefer:
- strong nouns and verbs
- concrete examples early
- operational texture
- measured confidence
- paragraph-driven development

Avoid:
- throat-clearing
- abstract managerial language
- inflated claims
- sentimentality
- faux profundity
- closing speeches

If a paragraph could fit unchanged into a random leadership newsletter, it is not specific enough.

If a post could have been written by someone without deep technical or operational experience, it is not grounded enough.

## Mechanics

- Use American English.
- Use the serial comma.
- Contractions are fine when natural.
- Em dashes are acceptable, but use them deliberately.
- Bold rarely. Italics only for light emphasis or titles.
- Keep capitalization conservative.
- Use numerals for dates, metrics, and operational detail; spell out where prose reads better.
- Keep quotations short unless the quoted language is unusually valuable.

## Hard Avoids

Do not publish writing that leans on:
- generic thought leadership
- unsupported opinion stated as settled fact
- vague praise of innovation, disruption, or vision
- management clichés
- broad generational stereotypes
- empty empathy language
- technology as branding rather than substance
- personal anecdotes treated as universal law

Be especially suspicious of prose that sounds polished, elevated, and empty. Credibility here should come from precision, judgment, and evidence, not tone alone.

## Editor Test

Before publishing, an editor should be able to answer yes to all of these:

1. Does this unmistakably sound like John rather than a composite business voice?
2. Is the piece grounded in a real situation, decision, pattern, or observation?
3. Does John's technical and operational background materially shape the thinking?
4. Are the claims supported by meaningful research when the topic calls for it?
5. Did the evidence challenge the draft anywhere, and was that handled honestly?
6. Is the lesson earned by the story or analysis rather than announced in advance?
7. Is the prose free of filler, cliché, and inflated language?
8. Does the post include a short `Further Reading` section when appropriate?

If any answer is no, the piece is not ready.

## AI Drafting Rules

An AI first draft should reduce John's cleanup work, not create it.

That means:
- start from a real situation, not a thesis statement
- write like a practitioner, not a coach or influencer
- make the technical background visible where relevant
- support claims with real evidence, not decorative references
- avoid generic leadership phrasing on sight
- keep the argument within honest scope
- leave the draft sounding like John, not like "professional content"

The agent's job is not to sound polished. The agent's job is to produce a draft that is specific, grounded, researched, and close enough to John's natural register that revision becomes sharpening rather than rewriting.

## Priority Order

When tradeoffs arise, prioritize:
1. Clarity
2. Honesty
3. Specificity
4. Evidence
5. Voice
6. Practical insight
7. Formal consistency

Apply the guide with judgment, not rigidity.
