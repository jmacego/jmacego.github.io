# AI vs. Dot-Com Series Outline

Working outline for the 5-post series.

Editorial constraints carried over from `STYLE_GUIDE.md`:
- Start from lived experience, not thesis-first abstraction.
- Move from situation to analysis to broader implication.
- Keep the voice direct, specific, and evidence-aware.
- Make John's technical and operational background materially visible.
- Use research to sharpen and discipline the argument, not to drown it.
- End published essays with a short `Further Reading` section.

## Series Through-Line

The dot-com boom was a bubble, but it also financed infrastructure, interfaces, behavior changes, and institutional learning that altered everyday life for decades. AI may follow a similar pattern: significant overinvestment, major destruction of capital, many individual losers, and a smaller number of durable technical and social changes that survive the crash.

This series is not pro-hype and not anti-technology. It is pro-judgment.

## Post 1

### Working Title

AI vs. Dot-Com: Bubbles Can Build Rails

### Purpose

Establish the thesis of the whole series:
- AI may be a bubble.
- That does not make it trivial.
- The market can be irrational while the underlying technological residue becomes foundational.
- The practical question for leaders is not whether they can stop the wave, but how to adapt without getting destroyed by it.

### Opening Scene

High school yearbook ad for JMac CGI Archive and JMaclabs.com.

Function of the scene:
- Establish John as an early operator and evangelist, not a late commentator.
- Connect the teenage internet-builder moment to the present-day identity without overplaying the callback.
- Show that internet belief looked strange, specific, and homemade before it became obvious.

### Sketch Outline

1. Opening: the yearbook ad, teenage builder culture, and the feeling that the internet was obviously better before most people agreed.
2. Early claim: for true believers, the mania felt early, but the aggregate market bubble came later.
3. Operator vantage: not one of the giant winners, but someone building real systems on a shoestring while hype machines scaled around him.
4. What the dot-com era got wrong:
   timing, profitability, hype, overbuild, and the gap between technical believers and mainstream users.
5. What it got right anyway:
   search, online payments, dark fiber, data centers, e-commerce habits, broadband, and logistics.
6. Pivot to AI:
   the same structure may be repeating in new form.
7. Strong claim:
   "This is the cheapest AI will be for quite a while."
8. What skeptics miss and what evangelists miss.
9. Executive takeaway:
   the goal is sustained relevance, growth, and profitability, not heroics.

### Full Working Outline

#### I. Opening: JMac in the Yearbook

- Scene: a high school yearbook ad for JMac CGI Archive and JMaclabs.com.
- The internet still feels niche, improvised, and legible mainly to the small cohort already inside it.
- John is not yet a detached observer; he is one of the teenagers building in public, convinced this is important before the broader world understands why.
- Transition line:
  the most dangerous and useful thing about technological revolutions is that the believers are often directionally right long before the market learns how to be sane about them.

#### II. The Early Internet Felt Manic Before the Market Peak

- Clarify scope honestly:
  there is a difference between what a technical subculture felt in 1995-1997 and what the aggregate stock market was doing by 1998-2000.
- Use research here to avoid sloppiness:
  DeLong and Magin are useful on the later aggregate bubble timing.
- John's point:
  from inside the early builder class, it already felt like everything was about to change.
- Supporting material from interview:
  link exchanges, web awards, CGI archives, logging tools, transparent pixel usage, DNS usability, small-ISP culture.

#### III. I Built Real Things, Not Just Stories

- Move into authority without sounding self-congratulatory.
- Describe the progression:
  software developer -> web hosting and dedicated server operator -> self-service provisioning and billing automation -> frontend to BIND -> later the failed effort to bring non-enthusiasts onto the internet through a brick-and-mortar office.
- Make one practical point:
  a lot of what is trivial now had to be invented as interface and workflow at the time.
- Strong parallel:
  making DNS accessible to normal people felt structurally similar to what ChatGPT has done for AI access.

#### IV. Where Operators and Hype Machines Split

- Contrast two types of participants:
  people building operating businesses versus people building narratives big enough to attract rich backers and public-market fantasy.
- John's experience:
  he built things he thought were real and scoped reasonably, which protected him from some of the worst implosions but also meant he missed the huge upside captured by hype merchants.
- Tone target:
  measured and sharp, not bitter.
- Possible line:
  some people got rich because they built real businesses; some got rich because they sold the impression that reality could wait.

#### V. The Useful Failure of 5guystech

- Present 5guystech as a case study in timing and adoption friction.
- Core diagnosis from interview:
  too late for the first wave of enthusiasts, too early for the second wave of real users.
- Specific errors:
  weak demand, compressed margins, in-person sales model, trying to persuade businesses who "should" use the internet before enough of them were ready.
- Broader lesson:
  being right too early is still a way of being wrong in business.

#### VI. What the Dot-Com Bubble Bought Anyway

- This is the heart of the article.
- Use ranked residue from interview, but organize it analytically:

1. Search
   - Search and discovery changed what it meant to find information, businesses, and products.
   - What had been directory-driven and artisanal became index-driven and scalable.

2. Online payments
   - Trust rails, checkout expectations, and merchant behavior changed.
   - E-commerce does not normalize without payment trust becoming boring.

3. Dark fiber and network overbuild
   - Overinvestment looked wasteful on investor balance sheets but lowered future constraints.
   - Later technical advances such as DWDM made excess capacity more valuable than people understood at the time.

4. Data centers and hosting structure
   - The hosting model, operational patterns, and infrastructure layers were already being shaped before cloud had a name.
   - This becomes the setup for later cloud economics and eventually AWS.

5. E-commerce habits
   - Consumers did not change overnight.
   - Mainstream behavior lagged the technology and required generational change.

6. Broadband and logistics
   - These deepen rather than start the story, but matter for later posts.

- Strong caution:
  public benefit and investor returns are not the same thing.

#### VII. The AI Parallel Is Real, but Not Simple

- Transition:
  AI today feels familiar not because it is identical to the internet, but because the structure rhymes.
- Similarities:
  true believers, interface simplification, subsidized use, overbuild, weak business models beside real utility, social anxiety, and opportunists trying to convert attention into capital.
- Distinguish infrastructure from applications:
  model tokens may commoditize; the durable residue may be elsewhere.

#### VIII. What AI May Be Building Under the Hype

- Candidate residues based on interview and research:
  power infrastructure, workflow harnesses, expert-augmentation habits, tooling layers, world models, and normalized AI-assisted analysis.
- Strong line from interview:
  "This is the cheapest AI will be for quite a while."
- Explore that claim carefully:
  current usage is heavily subsidized; a future crash may improve discipline but worsen pricing.
- Emphasize uncertainty honestly:
  we still do not know what parts of the current stack become foundational and which become scrap.

#### IX. What Skeptics Miss

- AI is not going away.
- Some jobs will disappear or compress.
- The affected categories may not match the original expectations.
- Refusing adaptation in the name of protecting existing moats can destroy industries.
- Tone guardrail:
  acknowledge pain without euphemism.

#### X. What Evangelists Miss

- Better does not automatically mean cheaper.
- General systems are not experts.
- AI works best as force multiplication for strong humans, not as proof that expertise no longer matters.
- Current business cases often fail because they are expensive, lower quality, and disliked by both customers and employees.

#### XI. The Executive Frame

- Most expensive current delusion:
  expensive solutions that customers do not like and employees are not using because they cost more, produce lower-quality results, and take more time.
- Practical guidance:
  stop chasing symbolic AI.
  focus on where quality, cost, adoption, and workflow all improve together.
- One-sentence close:
  every revolution has huge winners and huge losers; the objective is not to win the hype cycle, but to stay relevant, useful, and profitable through the one that comes after.

#### XII. Further Reading

- Keep to 3-4 items.
- Candidate set for post 1:
  DeLong and Magin
  Greenstein and Prince
  Bick, Blandin, and Deming
  Caballero or Acemoglu

## Post 2

### Working Title

Amazon, E-Commerce, and the Rewiring of Retail

### Purpose

Show how one of the most visible long-tail outcomes of the internet bubble was not just online shopping, but a complete rewiring of retail structure, customer expectation, price transparency, and store geography.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with the old assumption that stores were where commerce happened and the internet was a side channel.
2. Explain why early e-commerce looked narrow and fragile.
3. Track Amazon from books to platform to infrastructure and logistics machine.
4. Show that what changed retail was not one company alone, but search, payments, fulfillment, and habit formation working together.
5. Separate "Amazon killed malls" from the fuller truth:
   malls were also overbuilt, demographically mismatched, and exposed to broader retail shifts.
6. Use John's retail/operator perspective to analyze customer behavior versus executive myth.
7. Close with the AI parallel:
   current AI tools may also look narrow before they become embedded in broader behavior shifts.

## Post 3

### Working Title

From DVD Mail to Streaming: How the Internet Changed Waiting

### Purpose

Use the move from DVD-by-mail to streaming as a case study in how infrastructure, compression, broadband, licensing, and behavior all have to line up before a new model becomes normal.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with the weirdness of DVD-by-mail once upon a time feeling revolutionary.
2. Explain why it worked:
   convenience beat the old rental model even before streaming was ready.
3. Show what had to become true before streaming could take over:
   broadband, devices, licensing, CDNs, compression, habit changes.
4. Explain how streaming rewired expectations around access, ownership, and waiting.
5. Note the business-model carnage and the second-order effects on media.
6. Close with the AI parallel:
   the first winning interface may not be the final dominant model.

## Post 4

### Working Title

Smartphones Made the Internet Ambient

### Purpose

Argue that the internet's deepest social change may not have been the web browser or e-commerce, but the smartphone making networked computing constant, portable, and ordinary.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with a behavior that would have sounded absurd in 1999 but is now routine.
2. Track smartphone adoption from novelty to near universality.
3. Show how mobile changed navigation, communication, media, commerce, identity, and everyday coordination.
4. Distinguish the internet as a place from the internet as an ambient layer of life.
5. Include losses as well as gains:
   dependency, surveillance, attention capture, social rewiring.
6. Close with the AI parallel:
   today's chat interfaces may be primitive precursors to something that later becomes ambient.

## Post 5

### Working Title

Wireless Access and the Places That Skipped the Old Internet

### Purpose

Show how mobile and wireless access created real opportunity in developing countries, especially where legacy infrastructure was weak, while also staying honest about affordability, usage gaps, and uneven gains.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with the mistake of assuming everyone follows the same infrastructure sequence.
2. Explain mobile-first adoption and leapfrogging.
3. Cover mobile money, entrepreneurship, labor-market shifts, and access to services.
4. Distinguish coverage from meaningful use.
5. Show where the optimistic story breaks:
   affordability, literacy, device access, electricity, gender gaps, regulation.
6. Use this as the broadest societal post in the series.
7. Close with the AI parallel:
   the most important beneficiaries of a technology wave are not always the ones the first investors or evangelists had in mind.
