# AI vs. Dot-Com Series Outline

Working outline for the 5-post series.

Editorial constraints carried over from `STYLE_GUIDE.md`:
- Start from lived experience, not thesis-first abstraction.
- Move from situation to analysis to broader implication.
- Keep the voice direct, specific, and evidence-aware.
- Make John's technical and operational background materially visible.
- Use research to sharpen and discipline the argument, not to drown it.
- End published essays with a short `Further Reading` section.

## Series Through-Line

The dot-com boom was a bubble, but it also financed infrastructure, interfaces, behavior changes, and institutional learning that altered everyday life for decades. AI may follow a similar pattern: significant overinvestment, major destruction of capital, many individual losers, and a smaller number of durable technical and social changes that survive the crash.

This series is not pro-hype and not anti-technology. It is pro-judgment.

## Post 1

### Working Title

AI vs. Dot-Com: Bubbles Can Build Foundations

### Purpose

Establish the thesis of the whole series:
- AI may be a bubble.
- That does not make it trivial.
- The market can be irrational while the underlying technological residue becomes foundational.
- The practical question for leaders is not whether they can stop the wave, but how to adapt without getting destroyed by it.

### Opening Scene

High school yearbook ad for JMac CGI Archive and JMaclabs.com.

Function of the scene:
- Establish John as an early operator and evangelist, not a late commentator.
- Connect the teenage internet-builder moment to the present-day identity without overplaying the callback.
- Show that internet belief looked strange, specific, and homemade before it became obvious.

### Sketch Outline

1. Opening: the yearbook ad, teenage builder culture, and the feeling that the internet was obviously better before most people agreed.
2. Early claim: for true believers, the mania felt early, but the aggregate market bubble came later.
3. Operator vantage: not one of the giant winners, but someone building real systems on a shoestring while hype machines scaled around him.
4. What the dot-com era got wrong:
   timing, profitability, hype, overbuild, and the gap between technical believers and mainstream users.
5. What it got right anyway:
   search, online payments, dark fiber, data centers, e-commerce habits, broadband, and logistics.
6. Pivot to AI:
   the same structure may be repeating in new form.
7. Strong claim:
   "This is the cheapest AI will be for quite a while."
8. What skeptics miss and what evangelists miss.
9. Executive takeaway:
   the goal is sustained relevance, growth, and profitability, not heroics.

### Full Working Outline

#### I. Opening: JMac in the Yearbook

- Scene: a high school yearbook ad for JMac CGI Archive and JMaclabs.com.
- The internet still feels niche, improvised, and legible mainly to the small cohort already inside it.
- John is not yet a detached observer; he is one of the teenagers building in public, convinced this is important before the broader world understands why.
- Transition line:
  the most dangerous and useful thing about technological revolutions is that the believers are often directionally right long before the market learns how to be sane about them.

#### II. The Early Internet Felt Manic Before the Market Peak

- Clarify scope honestly:
  there is a difference between what a technical subculture felt in 1995-1997 and what the aggregate stock market was doing by 1998-2000.
- Use research here to avoid sloppiness:
  DeLong and Magin are useful on the later aggregate bubble timing.
- John's point:
  from inside the early builder class, it already felt like everything was about to change.
- Supporting material from interview:
  link exchanges, web awards, CGI archives, logging tools, transparent pixel usage, DNS usability, small-ISP culture.

#### III. I Built Real Things, Not Just Stories

- Move into authority without sounding self-congratulatory.
- Describe the progression:
  software developer -> web hosting and dedicated server operator -> self-service provisioning and billing automation -> frontend to BIND -> later the failed effort to bring non-enthusiasts onto the internet through a brick-and-mortar office.
- Make one practical point:
  a lot of what is trivial now had to be invented as interface and workflow at the time.
- Strong parallel:
  making DNS accessible to normal people felt structurally similar to what ChatGPT has done for AI access.

#### IV. Where Operators and Hype Machines Split

- Contrast two types of participants:
  people building operating businesses versus people building narratives big enough to attract rich backers and public-market fantasy.
- John's experience:
  he built things he thought were real and scoped reasonably, which protected him from some of the worst implosions but also meant he missed the huge upside captured by hype merchants.
- Tone target:
  measured and sharp, not bitter.
- Possible line:
  some people got rich because they built real businesses; some got rich because they sold the impression that reality could wait.

#### V. The Useful Failure of 5guystech

- Present 5guystech as a case study in timing and adoption friction.
- Core diagnosis from interview:
  too late for the first wave of enthusiasts, too early for the second wave of real users.
- Specific errors:
  weak demand, compressed margins, in-person sales model, trying to persuade businesses who "should" use the internet before enough of them were ready.
- Broader lesson:
  being right too early is still a way of being wrong in business.

#### VI. What the Dot-Com Bubble Bought Anyway

- This is the heart of the article.
- Use ranked residue from interview, but organize it analytically:

1. Search
   - Search and discovery changed what it meant to find information, businesses, and products.
   - What had been directory-driven and artisanal became index-driven and scalable.

2. Online payments
   - Trust rails, checkout expectations, and merchant behavior changed.
   - E-commerce does not normalize without payment trust becoming boring.

3. Dark fiber and network overbuild
   - Overinvestment looked wasteful on investor balance sheets but lowered future constraints.
   - Later technical advances such as DWDM made excess capacity more valuable than people understood at the time.

4. Data centers and hosting structure
   - The hosting model, operational patterns, and infrastructure layers were already being shaped before cloud had a name.
   - This becomes the setup for later cloud economics and eventually AWS.

5. E-commerce habits
   - Consumers did not change overnight.
   - Mainstream behavior lagged the technology and required generational change.

6. Broadband and logistics
   - These deepen rather than start the story, but matter for later posts.

- Strong caution:
  public benefit and investor returns are not the same thing.

#### VII. The AI Parallel Is Real, but Not Simple

- Transition:
  AI today feels familiar not because it is identical to the internet, but because the structure rhymes.
- Similarities:
  true believers, interface simplification, subsidized use, overbuild, weak business models beside real utility, social anxiety, and opportunists trying to convert attention into capital.
- Distinguish infrastructure from applications:
  model tokens may commoditize; the durable residue may be elsewhere.

#### VIII. What AI May Be Building Under the Hype

- Candidate residues based on interview and research:
  power infrastructure, workflow harnesses, expert-augmentation habits, tooling layers, world models, and normalized AI-assisted analysis.
- Strong line from interview:
  "This is the cheapest AI will be for quite a while."
- Explore that claim carefully:
  current usage is heavily subsidized; a future crash may improve discipline but worsen pricing.
- Emphasize uncertainty honestly:
  we still do not know what parts of the current stack become foundational and which become scrap.

#### IX. What Skeptics Miss

- AI is not going away.
- Some jobs will disappear or compress.
- The affected categories may not match the original expectations.
- Refusing adaptation in the name of protecting existing moats can destroy industries.
- Tone guardrail:
  acknowledge pain without euphemism.

#### X. What Evangelists Miss

- Better does not automatically mean cheaper.
- General systems are not experts.
- AI works best as force multiplication for strong humans, not as proof that expertise no longer matters.
- Current business cases often fail because they are expensive, lower quality, and disliked by both customers and employees.

#### XI. The Executive Frame

- Most expensive current delusion:
  expensive solutions that customers do not like and employees are not using because they cost more, produce lower-quality results, and take more time.
- Practical guidance:
  stop chasing symbolic AI.
  focus on where quality, cost, adoption, and workflow all improve together.
- One-sentence close:
  every revolution has huge winners and huge losers; the objective is not to win the hype cycle, but to stay relevant, useful, and profitable through the one that comes after.

#### XII. Further Reading

- Keep to 3-4 items.
- Candidate set for post 1:
  DeLong and Magin
  Greenstein and Prince
  Bick, Blandin, and Deming
  Caballero or Acemoglu

## Post 2

### Working Title

Amazon, E-Commerce, and the Rewiring of Retail

### Purpose

Show how one of the most visible long-tail outcomes of the internet bubble was not just online shopping, but a complete rewiring of retail structure, customer expectation, price transparency, and store geography — and draw the parallel explicitly to how AI is currently winning in narrow beachheads before expanding.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with the old assumption that stores were where commerce happened and the internet was a side channel.
   AI parallel threaded here: the same assumption surrounds AI today — it's the side channel, the novelty, not the main event. Yet.
2. Explain why early e-commerce looked narrow and fragile.
   AI parallel: current AI tools also look narrow — good at coding assistance, document drafting, customer service scripts. That narrowness is not permanent; it is the beachhead.
3. Track Amazon from books to platform to infrastructure and logistics machine.
   AI parallel at each stage: ChatGPT started in a narrow category (text generation); now AI is expanding into agents, workflows, and infrastructure. The storefront may not be the durable layer.
4. Show that what changed retail was not one company alone, but search, payments, fulfillment, and habit formation working together.
   AI parallel: the current ecosystem of models, APIs, orchestration layers, and workflow integrations is the equivalent. No single company wins alone; the stack has to line up.
5. Prime rewired customer expectations — delivery speed became a baseline, not a premium.
   AI parallel: once enough workers use AI-assisted drafting, analysis, or coding daily, the expectation of that speed and assistance becomes the new baseline. Competitors not meeting it will feel slow, not just different.
6. Separate "Amazon killed malls" from the fuller truth:
   malls were also overbuilt, demographically mismatched, and exposed to broader retail shifts.
   AI parallel: "AI will replace all knowledge workers" is the equivalent oversimplification. Exposed categories were already fragile. Strong practitioners will reorganise, not disappear.
7. What good operators did: they identified what their format could do that fulfillment centres cannot.
   AI parallel: the equivalent question for every professional and business is not "can AI do this?" but "what do I do that AI genuinely cannot, and am I building from there?"
8. Close with the through-line: the rewiring of retail took two decades and required search, payments, logistics, and generational habit change to all align. We do not know yet which combination of factors will make AI embedded behavior rather than an interesting tool. But we can watch for the alignment signals.

## Post 3

### Working Title

From DVD Mail to Streaming: How the Internet Changed Waiting

### Purpose

Use the move from DVD-by-mail to streaming as a case study in how infrastructure, compression, broadband, licensing, and behavior all have to line up before a new model becomes normal — and run that checklist against the current AI stack to show what still has to align.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with the weirdness of DVD-by-mail once upon a time feeling revolutionary.
   AI parallel threaded here: current AI interfaces will look equally transitional in retrospect. The question is what they are the transitional phase toward.
2. Explain why DVD-by-mail worked:
   convenience beat the old rental model even before streaming was ready. It solved real friction with the tools available, not the ideal ones.
   AI parallel: current AI tools are the DVD-by-mail stage. They solve real friction imperfectly. That imperfection does not mean they lose — it means the better version is still being built underneath.
3. Show what had to become true before streaming could take over:
   broadband, devices, licensing, CDNs, compression, habit changes. None of these were guaranteed. Each took time and capital.
   AI parallel: run the checklist explicitly. What does AI need before it becomes as embedded as streaming? Candidates: reliable latency, lower inference cost, workflow integration, trust, regulation, and the generation that grows up using it as a given. Which of these are already aligning? Which are still years out?
4. Explain how streaming rewired expectations around access, ownership, and waiting.
   AI parallel: once AI drafting or analysis becomes fast and reliable enough, the expectation of having it available will rewire what "normal" work pace means. The people who never had it will seem to be working with one hand tied.
5. Note the business-model carnage: content economics were destroyed and rebuilt. Blockbuster failed not because streaming was inevitable from day one but because it could not adapt once the conditions aligned.
   AI parallel: the parallel for knowledge-work industries is direct. The conditions for disruption are assembling. The timeline is uncertain. The direction is not.
6. The first winning interface is not the final dominant model.
   Netflix DVD was not Netflix streaming. ChatGPT may not be the final form of how people interact with AI. Something more ambient, more integrated, more invisible may be the actual endpoint.
7. Close with the honest uncertainty: we do not know which AI products survive to become infrastructure the way streaming did. But we can identify which preconditions are still missing — and track when they start to close.

## Post 4

### Working Title

Smartphones Made the Internet Ambient

### Purpose

Argue that the internet's deepest social change was not the browser or e-commerce but the smartphone making networked computing constant, portable, and invisible — and ask directly whether AI will follow the same path from tool to ambient layer.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with a behavior that would have sounded absurd in 1999 but is now so routine it is invisible.
   AI parallel threaded immediately: name a specific AI-assisted behavior that sounds slightly absurd today but may be equally invisible in fifteen years. Make it concrete.
2. Track smartphone adoption from novelty to near universality.
   AI parallel at each stage: current AI adoption curve versus smartphone curve. Where are we on that timeline? What signals suggest acceleration or stall?
3. Show how mobile changed navigation, communication, media, commerce, identity, and everyday coordination.
   AI parallel: go category by category. Navigation → AI routing decisions. Communication → AI-drafted messages. Commerce → AI-assisted purchasing and negotiation. Identity → how does AI change how we present expertise and competence? Each of these is a real question, not a rhetorical one.
4. The smartphone created entirely new categories of behavior that nobody predicted — Uber, Instagram, dating apps — not just faster versions of old behaviors.
   AI parallel: the most important AI applications may not exist yet. The ones that look most powerful now may not be the ones that reshape daily life. Maintain genuine uncertainty about which applications become the Uber of AI.
5. Distinguish the internet as a place from the internet as an ambient layer of life.
   AI parallel: today AI is a place you go — you open a chat window. The ambient version would be AI woven into every surface you already work on. That transition, if it happens, is the equivalent of the smartphone shift.
6. Include losses as well as gains:
   dependency, surveillance, attention capture, social rewiring. Do not skip this.
   AI parallel: what are the equivalent risks when AI becomes ambient? Dependency on systems you cannot audit. Homogenisation of output. The erosion of the friction that used to force people to develop judgment. These are not hypothetical.
7. Close: the smartphone question for AI is not whether AI gets better. It is whether it gets invisible. Invisible is what changes society, not impressive.

## Post 5

### Working Title

Wireless Access and the Places That Skipped the Old Internet

### Purpose

Show how mobile and wireless access created real opportunity in developing countries by leapfrogging legacy infrastructure — and ask directly whether AI will compress expert-access gaps across health, law, finance, and education in the same way, while staying honest about where those gains will not arrive automatically.

### Sketch Outline

1. Open with the mistake of assuming everyone follows the same infrastructure sequence.
   AI parallel threaded immediately: the assumption that AI adoption follows a rich-world, office-worker, knowledge-professional track may be as wrong as assuming mobile adoption would follow the landline track.
2. Explain mobile-first adoption and leapfrogging.
   AI parallel: countries and communities without deep expert infrastructure in medicine, law, or financial services may adopt AI-assisted versions of those services faster and more completely than wealthy countries locked into existing professional structures. The leapfrog may be sharper.
3. Cover mobile money, entrepreneurship, labor-market shifts, and access to services.
   AI parallel: mobile money (M-Pesa) gave people access to financial infrastructure they had been structurally excluded from. What does the equivalent look like for AI? A farmer getting crop-disease diagnosis. A patient in a low-resource clinic getting a second opinion. A first-generation student getting the kind of guidance that prep-school kids take for granted. These are not science fiction — early versions exist. The question is whether they scale and hold quality.
4. Distinguish coverage from meaningful use.
   AI parallel: access to an AI tool is not the same as benefit from an AI tool. The same gaps that limited mobile's reach — cost, literacy, electricity, language support, cultural fit — apply to AI. "AI is available" is not the same as "AI is useful here."
5. Show where the optimistic story breaks:
   affordability, literacy, device access, electricity, gender gaps, regulation.
   AI parallel: run each directly. Inference cost in low-income contexts. Language model quality in minority languages. Regulatory environments that restrict AI medical or legal advice. The gender gap in technology access that mobile never fully closed.
6. Use this as the broadest societal post in the series — zoom out to ask who gets to benefit and on what timeline.
   The dot-com bubble was largely a wealthy-world, English-language event. Mobile was more democratic than that but still uneven. AI could be more democratic still — or could amplify existing inequalities if access, quality, and affordability are not addressed.
7. Close: the most important beneficiaries of this wave may not be the knowledge workers in San Francisco or London who are currently most anxious about it. They may be in places that never had access to the kind of expertise AI can approximate — and for whom an imperfect AI doctor or lawyer is not a downgrade from the real thing, but an upgrade from nothing.

### Pre-Draft Interview Required

**Do not draft Post 5 without first interviewing John.** He has direct operator experience from Africa in the early 2000s that the published research memos cannot reproduce and that this post will be weak without. Specific threads to draw out in the interview:

- Tanzania and the absence of copper infrastructure. Home phones were cellular from the start because there was nothing to retrofit — what did that look like on the ground, how did it shape expectations, and what broke when the assumption of landline-first did not apply?
- Satellite connectivity in the same period. Same physical satellites that were available in Europe, but local regulation forced the use of approved domestic contractors and service providers at dramatic markups. What was the multiplier, how did that rent structure shape who could actually get online, and what parallels does John see to current AI regulatory and access friction in comparable markets?
- Specific operators, businesses, or customers John worked with whose trajectories illustrate the leapfrog and its limits.
- What the "mobile-first" framing gets right, and what it misses, from someone who was actually there rather than reading about it afterwards.
- How this period shaped John's view of the difference between coverage and meaningful use — the conceptual spine of the post.
- Any parallels John sees between the 2000s satellite-contractor rent-seeking and current AI access patterns in regulated sectors or lower-income markets.

The interview should happen before research is synthesised into prose. John's vantage is the argument's load-bearing column, and the research exists to sharpen and discipline that vantage, not to substitute for it.

- Save the deeper Amazon, streaming, smartphone, and development examples for the later essays.

- John should appear in every essay, but the ratio of autobiography to analysis can vary.

  measured, sharp, skeptical of hype, but not cynical about technology itself.
