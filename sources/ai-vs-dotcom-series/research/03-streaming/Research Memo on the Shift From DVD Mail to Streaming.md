# Research Memo on the Shift From DVD Mail to Streaming

## Executive summary

The shift from DVD-by-mail to streaming was not a single technological leap. It was a staged transition in which the internet first improved the economics and convenience of physical media, and only later became good enough to replace it at scale. Netflix began operations on April 14, 1998. By April 18, 2007, it had about 6.8 million subscribers; by then the business model that worked was still overwhelmingly physical: a large DVD catalog, queue management, no late fees, and a distribution network that could reach more than 90% of subscribers with generally one-business-day delivery. In other words, the early internet did not yet make video delivery easy, but it did make discovery, ordering, and logistics dramatically better than store rental. Streaming became the default model only after several separate conditions converged. Household broadband adoption rose from 47% of U.S. adults in early 2007 to 66% in May 2010 and 70% in May 2013. At the same time, living-room internet access improved: 24% of U.S. households had at least one TV connected to the internet in 2010, rising to 38% in 2012 and 49% in 2014. Connection quality also improved materially; one widely cited 2013 speed benchmark put average U.S. connection speed at 8.7 Mbps. Those shifts mattered because NetflixŌĆÖs service only became habit-forming when it moved from the PC to the television, and from intermittent buffering to reliable enough playback. The companyŌĆÖs own metrics show the crossover. Netflix launched its first streaming option in January 2007. By the fourth quarter of 2009, 48% of subscribers had watched more than 15 minutes of streaming content. ## Key findings

1. **DVD-by-mail worked because it solved a different problem than streaming.** In the late 1990s and 2000s, the internet was far better at search, recommendations, queue management, and fulfillment coordination than it was at reliable mass video delivery. Netflix could offer a much broader catalog than a physical store and combine it with one-day mail turnaround for most subscribers. That was enough to beat the trip-to-the-store model years before broadband could beat the disc itself. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn22view4Ņłéturn24view0Ņłéturn21view0Ņłü

2. **Early streaming was a supplement, not a substitute.** Even after the 2007 launch of Watch Now, Netflix still treated the combined streaming-plus-DVD offer as its core strategy. In its 2009 10-K, the company said the bundle created a ŌĆ£uniquely compelling selectionŌĆØ relative to streaming-only services. This is important because many retrospective accounts compress the period and imply that streaming was obviously ready to take over as soon as it appeared. It was not. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn25view0Ņłéturn23search15Ņłü

3. **Rights constraints delayed the transition more than many commentators admit.** NetflixŌĆÖs 2008 annual report states flatly that streaming, unlike DVD, was not subject to the first-sale doctrine and depended entirely on licensor permission. Studios could withdraw titles quickly, carve up rights by window and by channel, and reserve key output deals for premium networks. That meant the DVD business retained real strategic value because its catalog was broader, steadier, and legally simpler. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn24view0Ņłü

4. **Broadband adoption mattered, but adoption alone was not enough.** U.S. home broadband moved from 47% of adults in early 2007 to 66% in 2010 and 70% in 2013. That is the right macro backdrop, but those numbers alone do not explain the crossover. The experience also had to be stable enough on real consumer networks to support sustained viewing rather than novelty use. ŅłĆciteŅłéturn3search12Ņłéturn4view1Ņłéturn3search9Ņłü

### What had to become true technically and behaviorally before streaming could replace physical media at scale

**Technically, four things had to become true at the same time.** First, broadband needed to be widespread enough in the home to make streaming normal rather than occasional. Second, compression and HTTP adaptive streaming needed to make video resilient on fluctuating real-world networks. Third, the service had to reach the television through connected devices and built-in apps, not just the PC. Fourth, Netflix had to build delivery architecture capable of handling massive, highly concentrated evening demand through CDNs, ISP interconnects, and then Open Connect deployments. **Behaviorally, four things also had to change.** Consumers had to accept access over possession, subscription over per-title rental, library breadth that was ŌĆ£broad enoughŌĆØ rather than complete, and the idea that the algorithm could help decide what to watch. They also had to stop treating computer playback as secondary and start treating TV-based app playback as normal. Finally, watching several episodes in a row had to feel like freedom rather than excess. The Netflix/OTT binge data from 2013 shows that this behavioral transition was already well underway by then. ## Tensions or contradictions in the evidence

- **What Netflix believed in 2009 and what later proved true were not the same.** In 2009 the company argued that combining streaming and DVD created a strong competitive advantage over streaming-only services. That was true in the narrow sense that the bundle compensated for streamingŌĆÖs incomplete catalog. But by late 2011, fewer than half of streaming subscribers also took DVD, and only about 7% of new members were choosing the hybrid offer. The bundle was an effective bridge, not the end state. - **What studios believed in the late 2000s and what later proved true also diverged.** Early streaming deals were often priced as ancillary revenue because they seemed unlikely to disrupt premium windows or physical sales. - **The postal system was part of the streaming story.** Before the internet could deliver video well, it let Netflix orchestrate physical media better than stores could. ## How this helps the series argument

This material supports a sharper thesis than ŌĆ£better technology beat an old format.ŌĆØ The stronger argument is that the internet first made physical media radically more convenient, then made physical media unnecessary only after broadband, connected TVs, adaptive delivery, licensing strategy, and subscription pricing all aligned. That framing lets the essay separate infrastructure from adoption, adoption from behavior, and behavior from investor outcomes. It also avoids the common mistake of treating 2007 streaming as if it were already the same thing as 2013 streaming. The most defensible series-level claim is that dominant media models do not flip when a new technology becomes possible. They flip when a bundle of technical conditions and consumer habits makes the old friction look irrational. In this case, the winning sequence was queue first, stream second, originals third.
