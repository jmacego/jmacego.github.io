## Tensions or contradictions in the evidence

- **Rapid user adoption versus slow enterprise transformation.** People adopt AI tools quickly, but firms change workflows, incentives, governance, and training much more slowly. - **Falling token prices versus rising system costs.** Token prices have collapsed on the commodity end, but electricity, depreciation, lease rates, integration effort, and premium frontier capacity are all moving in the other direction. ## How this helps the series argument

The sharpest series argument is probably this: AI is like the dot-com era in one narrow but important sense, capital spending is racing ahead of fully proven business models, while prices at the commodity edge are being competed down. But it is unlike the dot-com era in distribution, because AI is arriving through existing clouds, apps, and workflows rather than waiting for households and firms to build broad new network access from scratch. That is why adoption can look faster even while economic reorganization still looks slow. ţłÇciteţłéturn37view0ţłéturn6view1ţłéturn20view1ţłü

A second useful argument is that commentators keep comparing one layer of the stack to another. The commodity layer is already under heavy price pressure. The defensible layer is increasingly power access, datacenter siting, customer distribution, workflow embedding, and internal monetization through existing businesses like advertising and cloud. That lets the writer avoid both hype and dismissal. It also gives a clean explanation for why some firms can justify todayÔÇÖs capex and others may not. ţłÇciteţłéturn18search0ţłéturn26view0ţłéturn28view0ţłéturn9view3ţłéturn34search0ţłéturn34search1ţłéturn35search2ţłéturn33view0ţłü

It is weak as a statement about low-end API inference, where the evidence still points toward compression.
