## Series Goals

- Build a 5-post series around the argument that a bubble can still finance durable social and technical change.
- Treat AI and the dot-com era as structurally comparable without pretending they are identical.
- Keep the tone hard-hitting, opinionated, evidence-aware, and grounded in lived experience.
- Maintain John's authority in every post through first-hand vantage, operational experience, and explicit judgment.
- Target roughly 3,000 words per post.
- Use one specific deep-dive topic per article.
- Work through repeated interview rounds and revisions rather than trying to generate final prose too early.

## Working Through-Line

Core thesis:

The dot-com boom was a bubble, but what came out of it was the internet-inflected world we now take for granted. AI may follow a similar pattern: enormous overinvestment, many losers, a smaller number of durable winners, and a set of infrastructure, workflow, and social changes that still reshape everyday life long after the market narrative breaks.

Desired practical message:

Every revolution will have huge winners and huge losers. The path is finding how to adapt, how to ensure you and your company are making the changes to ride that wave, not to huge wins or losses but to sustained relevance, growth, and profitability.

## Interview Round 1

Date: 2026-04-18

### Prompt Context

The goal of this round was to establish the first post's thesis and capture John's lived vantage on the dot-com era, AI, and the relationship between bubbles, infrastructure, and durable value.

### Raw Answers

#### 1. Where were you positioned during the dot-com run-up, specifically from 1995 to 2001, and what did you see from the infrastructure side that most later commentators miss?

From 1995 to 2001: I first was CEO of a moderately successful web hosting and dedicated server provider. Then a self-funded (and unsuccessful) hardware-less competitor that focused on services and internet adoption for businesses. I ended 2001 having pivoted to partnering in a media company and starting an aviation business to get out of technology all together being quite done with it. I got to first build the infrastructure, pivoting from software developer to running a company that first built on other people's hardware and then started, on a shoestring, deploying our own. We weren't one of the companies that everyone threw money at, but more the one that the local news picked up as scrappy teenagers ahead of the curve. It gave a very interesting vantage on what worked, and then what horribly did not. Although I learned a ton at 5guystech even from the failure.

#### 2. What is the clearest memory you have of the moment the internet economy stopped feeling ambitious and started feeling manic?

I was a teenager. I was going to be a doctor like my step father Wilson Leake and follow in the general medical footsteps of my mother, an OR RN, and Wilson's father Chauncey Depue Leake. Computers came naturally for me, but until a tiny ISP started I had to pay long distance charges to even get AOL. Silverlink was the tiny ISP, and I became jmac, all lower case, because they taught me more of how BBS and the real start of the internet worked.

It almost felt, at least for that small cohort of people, that it was manic from the moment I got involved. I ran a link exchange system, myself and other teenagers reviewed websites and offered tiers of awards with links back to us. It was a marketing campaign that was also somewhat useful. This was at the time of link directories and before AltaVista was even good and Google wasn't a twinkle. I created log metrics servers for other users. I believe I was the first to use a transparent pixel. I advertised my CGI script repo in my high school yearbook.

But we were all still building things, mostly useful things. Then it became a what can the internet make better, easier, etc. And, I'll be honest, I was one of the ones that thought it was everything. I was an evangelist. And I couldn't possibly understand why friends, my parents, their friends, the generation older than me, and so on just simply didn't understand why this was better. And of course, that was the sort of person, like AI today, the true believers that drove a lot of the hype that others capitalized on.

Yes, I'm semi-retired now, relatively young, but I'm not a billionaire. I didn't really strike it rich. I'm traveling the world and I live comfortably, but I was one of the people that the real money was built on without actually receiving it. I didn't position myself well enough, and I didn't get on the bandwagons of things I didn't believe in.

#### 3. What did investors, founders, or operators keep getting wrong then, even when they were directionally right that the internet mattered?

Investors, founders, operators, and people like myself got a variety of things wrong, and in different categories. I was a real operating believer, so I built real things, or things I thought were real, scoped reasonably. So I got left behind by the people who built up hype and pumped and dumped. But I came out ahead of the people who either believed their own hype or believed the hype of others.

I'm trying very hard to take those lessons to AI. Which is why I've waited so long to write this article series. Part of me, honestly, wants to get in on the hype and make a billion dollars. I'm not saying anyone involved in AI, or crypto before it, are corrupt. I'm not not saying that, though, of course. But I tend to like the quote: "Everyone either wants less corruption or more opportunity to participate in it."

We all want easy money. That's natural. And we all want the world to be fair. Neither of those are going to happen to the vast majority of us.

We were directionally right, but too far ahead. We built too much too fast that couldn't make money. I did some. I watched a lot of other people do even more. Some got rich, much more so than me. Some got poor.

#### 4. What durable things did the bubble fund anyway?

As someone who has been in network ops for the last 10 years, definitely all the dark fiber. Making that so cheap, and it took DWDM really, random advances in technology that were adjacent and we couldn't predict, is a huge aspect of these things.

We also built the structure of hosting, datacenters, and so on. That eventually became AWS when Amazon realized it was more profitable to sell infrastructure than books, but the public funded the books, the hype. But even then that was such a gamble.

"If you put $1 into Amazon you'd have $X today!" is a common refrain, but if you put a reasonable amount into a basket of tech companies, you'd have done about as well as the S&P 500. Most people lost money.

The habits and the consumer behavior were harder and took longer. Gen X and then Millennials had to grow up with the change and then become the consumers for it to really, really become mainstream. The people who adopted faster did best, but as a whole for society it took a generational change in spending.

#### 5 and 6. What is the closest AI equivalent right now, and where is current AI capital allocation irrational?

The closest AI equivalent is odd. GPU capacity is a one to two year cycle on a five to six year amortization. That won't last. The data centers I think are at risk of going bust. Maybe they are dark fiber. Hard to say.

I think the power infrastructure build is the closest to dark fiber. So many sci-fi authors, especially Kardashev-scale stuff, point to the consumption and distribution of power to be the tiering of a society. But it's all hidden. Hormuz has really showcased just how much of the world's industrial output, economic output, and social output is entirely based on the smooth and continuous flow of oil for power generation. Data centers are showcasing just what levels that can scale to, and in different locations, and in many ways using different, or at least investigating, or causing the discussion of alternatives, to oil and gas. Pipelines, LNG facilities, wind farms, solar farms, nuclear plants are all super long term investments and are being forced.

Foundation models are interesting, but tokens are becoming a commodity. Claude Opus, ChatGPT, Deepseek R4 are fairly competitive from a token standpoint, but at potentially drastically different costs. And right now all those tokens are being hugely subsidized, which isn't sustainable.

But the harnesses are becoming very interesting. That covers tooling, data labeling, agents, workflows, and so on. That money subsidizing tokens is driving that other development. It's also driving world models, which I still find extremely interesting.

AI today without a doubt does a job, and does it reasonably well. We don't really know what that job is, and we especially don't really know how much that job is going to cost. Everyone says, "This is the worst AI will ever be," and I don't entirely agree. This is the cheapest AI will be for quite a while. Probably when the money dries up the real cost will exist.

We'll retreat to cheaper models for a time. We'll stop using expensive models for things it's cheaper for a human to do. And they are so subsidized that yes, that's a category. Business process outsourcing is at risk, but it may not be dead. Sorry, I'm in Southeast Asia, so BPO is a big deal here.

I don't necessarily think it's irrational. I think it's driving innovation. It's doing it very, very expensively, and a lot of people are going to lose shirts, but it is driving forward.

#### 7. What do AI skeptics get wrong?

Lots of people dismiss AI completely, especially those who are trying to build moats around certain job types. Sorry, AI is not going away. Many jobs are going away. That happens regularly and society adapts, more jobs are created, and so on. The jobs being impacted aren't the ones we expected, either. And I am very, very sorry for anyone who loses their job over this. It sucks. And we shouldn't paper over that.

But AI is here, it does many things well, and we are still trying to figure out what it does well and what it does poorly. It's going to change the way we work across many fields that no one expects. And standing in the way with moats is going to destroy whole industries instead of letting them ride the currents and adapt.

It also has the potential to reshape the landscape of what cities, states, and countries are, depending on how it is used and regulated.

#### 8. What do AI evangelists get wrong?

I touched on it earlier, but that it will get cheaper and better. It might, but I think that's unlikely. Better, sure. But mostly what will improve is how we use it, what we figure out it can do better, how we build around that.

I see so many people, and news articles and such, that hold AI up as better, more intelligent, more accurate than a human. Because in many ways really good AI, when well harnessed and prompted, is better than an average human at everything. But rarely is it ever better than an expert in a specific field. It's general. It does synergize well with experts.

An expert lead developer can honestly shepherd a dozen agents to do more work faster than they can a small dev team. But the AI cannot lead that dev team right now. The same is true in most fields. It synergizes well as an aid to doctors. The stats are pretty shocking on accuracy versus a doctor. But it can only analyze that gathered information, and it needs help. It's a great second opinion for almost any field. It's a great first opinion in many with an expert human as a second.

It takes the top people in their fields and can augment them to 10x. It also takes the average person and, used well and steered by others, augments their ability with expert-level assistance. And please keep in mind I am always a fan of the average person. We put too many experts, and I include myself here, up on pedestals. And too many of us believe we are experts in all fields.

Especially people who truly are experts in some field: particle physics doesn't necessarily make you a good cook, and a minimum wage line cook can almost certainly out cook you because they are the expert in their field.

#### 9. If the AI market broke hard in the next 12 to 24 months, what would still be standing 10 years later?

Many areas have been reshaped and there's no going back. Doctors should always be AI augmented. Data analysis should always include querying an LLM.

"You should learn to code!" was wrong when it started, and AI has killed that. Almost no jobs have ever needed coding. What they need is a process mindset that is often associated with coding and people have no idea how to say that. This needs to be an article.

Customer service, front of house staff, secretaries, greeters, support, and so on have always been hard pressed in high wage countries, and this has just put another series of nails in those coffins and that isn't coming back. BPO is, while not dead, at risk.

#### 10. What is the one sentence you want a serious executive reader to remember after finishing post 1?

Every revolution will have huge winners and huge losers. I believe the path is finding how to adapt, how to ensure you and your company are making the changes to ride that wave, not to huge wins or losses but to sustained relevance, growth, and profitability.

## Emerging Draft Angles

- Dot-com mania did not begin everywhere at once; for technical believers it could feel manic very early, but DeLong-style market dating may still place the true aggregate bubble later.
- The bubble funded real infrastructure even where most investors lost money.
- Social adoption lagged far behind technical possibility.
- AI may repeat the pattern, but the durable residue may be power, workflow infrastructure, and expert-augmentation habits more than today's subsidized token economics.
- The "cheapest AI" framing is potentially more revealing than the common "worst AI" framing.

## Research Spine: Round 1

These are early anchor sources for the series. This list is selective rather than exhaustive and will keep growing.

### Dot-Com Bubble and Diffusion

- DeLong and Magin, "A Short Note on the Size of the Dot-Com Bubble" (NBER, 2006)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w12011
  - Useful corrective: they argue there is little sign of a significant aggregate U.S. stock market bubble until roughly 1998, even though many participants experienced the culture as manic much earlier.

- Greenstein and Prince, "The Diffusion of the Internet and the Geography of the Digital Divide in the United States" (NBER, 2006)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w12182
  - Useful for separating technical availability, adoption, and uneven geographic diffusion.

- Goolsbee, "The Value of Broadband and the Deadweight Loss of Taxing New Technology" (NBER, 2006)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w11994
  - Useful for showing how early-stage diffusion dynamics and delayed market entry materially affect welfare.

- Greenstein and McDevitt, "The Broadband Bonus: Accounting for Broadband Internet's Impact on U.S. GDP" (NBER, 2009)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w14758
  - Useful for quantifying some of the durable economic residue of broadband buildout from 1999 to 2006.

### Retail, E-Commerce, and Market Structure

- Goldmanis, Hortacsu, Syverson, and Emre, "E-commerce and the Market Structure of Retail Industries" (NBER, 2008)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w14166
  - Useful claim: lower search costs tend to reallocate market share from lower-type firms to higher-type firms.

- Chava, Oettl, Singh, and Zeng, "Creative Destruction? Impact of E-commerce on the Retail Sector" summary (NBER Digest, 2022)
  - https://www.nber.org/digest/202208/effect-e-commerce-expansion-local-retail
  - Useful numbers: online sales grew from 0.63% of total retail sales in 1999 to 13.3% in 2021; a major e-commerce retailer had 3 fulfillment centers in 2000 and more than 90 by the end of 2016.

- U.S. Census Bureau, Quarterly Retail E-Commerce Sales Report
  - https://www.census.gov/retail/ecommerce.html
  - Useful current benchmark: e-commerce accounted for 16.6% of total U.S. retail sales in Q4 2025.

### AI Adoption and Economic Framing

- Bick, Blandin, and Deming, "The Rapid Adoption of Generative AI" (NBER, 2024; rev. 2025)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w32966
  - Useful numbers: by late 2024, nearly 40% of the U.S. population age 18 to 64 used generative AI; 23% of employed respondents had used it for work in the previous week; 9% used it every work day.

- Acemoglu, "The Simple Macroeconomics of AI" (NBER, 2024)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487
  - Useful brake on hype: macro effects may be meaningful but modest under plausible assumptions, with no more than a 0.66% increase in TFP over 10 years in the paper's baseline framing.

- Caballero, "Speculative Growth and the AI 'Bubble'" (NBER, 2026)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w34722
  - Useful conceptual bridge: AI may generate "rational but fragile" speculative-growth equilibria in which elevated valuations finance real capital accumulation, yet remain vulnerable to a confidence break.

### Smartphones and Mobile Access

- Pew Research Center, Mobile Fact Sheet (2025)
  - https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/fact-sheet/mobile/
  - Useful numbers: 91% of U.S. adults owned a smartphone in 2025, up from 35% in Pew's first smartphone survey in 2011. Also useful for "smartphone-only" access patterns.

- GSMA, State of Mobile Internet Connectivity 2024
  - https://www.gsma.com/r/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/The-State-of-Mobile-Internet-Connectivity-Report-2024.pdf
  - Useful numbers from GSMA summary: by end-2023, 4.6 billion people were using mobile internet on their own device; 3.1 billion people lived within coverage but did not use it; 350 million remained outside coverage.

- Chiplunkar and Goldberg, "The Employment Effects of Mobile Internet in Developing Countries" (NBER, 2022)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w30741
  - Useful for tying mobile internet access to labor-force participation, sectoral shifts, and entrepreneurship.

- Jack and Suri, "Mobile Money: The Economics of M-PESA" (NBER, 2011)
  - https://www.nber.org/papers/w16721
  - Useful for showing how mobile connectivity became financial infrastructure, not just communications infrastructure.

- World Bank, Global Findex 2025 / Digital Connectivity Tracker
  - https://www.worldbank.org/en/publication/globalfindex
  - Useful current global framing around mobile phone ownership, smartphone access, and digital finance.

## Interview Round 2

Date: 2026-04-18

### Purpose

Sharpen the opening scene, establish the strongest parallels for post 1, and identify where the series should be measured versus combative.

### Raw Answers

#### 1. Best opening scene for post 1

Teenage internet builder moment. Best parallel to the evangelists today.

#### 2. Concrete "real things" built in that era

- Frontend to BIND, making DNS accessible to regular people. This reminds me a lot of ChatGPT.
- Portal and automation to tie billing to self-service hosting account provisioning. We take this so for granted today.
- A real brick-and-mortar office for an internet company, 5guystech.
- Trying to bridge the gap between all the people who should use the internet and weren't.
- Biggest waste of money: it didn't work.

#### 3. What 5guystech got wrong

5guystech got most things wrong.

- Timing: too late for the first wave of tech enthusiasts, too early for the second wave of real users.
- That led to the rest: poor demand, compressed margins, and a terrible sales model.
- Sales model: in person, "come sit at an office and describe your web needs."
- Without hugely capitalized market hype, that model would not work, and probably still would have just been a money sink.

#### 4. What the hype machine looked like

The hype machine folks at the time were the ones who mostly talked wealthy friends into bankrolling something, to get their name out, to appear bigger than life, to get a VC or similar to take it public with massively negative earnings. That sounds familiar.

#### 5. Ranking of durable residue from the dot-com era for post 1

1. search
2. online payments
3. dark fiber
4. data centers
5. e-commerce
6. broadband
7. logistics

#### 6. Most expensive current AI delusion

Expensive solutions that customers don't like and employees aren't using because it costs more, produces lower quality results, and takes more time.

#### 7. What the AI boom may buy even if it crashes

- A significant, for good or bad, impact to creative fields. I think they are hit harder than technical ones.
- An improved power grid.
- A very cynical view of hype trains for a while, because more people have gotten into AI than crypto, so the market impact will be bigger and more people are into stocks.

#### 8. Desired rhetorical edge

Measured and sharp. Tone down the overly combative edges.
